## 3.1 Overview of the Research Approach

This diagnostic study employed a structured, multi-method research approach designed to produce actionable findings at the value chain level. The methodology combined desk-based analysis with primary data collection, ensuring that findings reflect both the published evidence base and the on-the-ground realities of Uganda's manufacturing sector.

The study was preceded by an Inception Report that documented the study design, diagnostic framework, data plan, and stakeholder consultation plan. The Inception Report was reviewed and approved by the Technical Working Group (TWG) prior to the diagnostic phase. This chapter summarizes the methodology as executed; the full methodological detail, including the diagnostic framework instruments and data plan, is contained in the Inception Report.

The research was conducted in four sequential phases:

1. **Desk review and secondary data compilation**
2. **Primary data collection — key informant interviews and field visits**
3. **Value chain analysis and prioritization scoring**
4. **Validation and finalization**

---

## 3.2 Desk Review and Secondary Data Compilation

The desk review established the evidence base for each value chain prior to primary data collection. Sources reviewed included:

- **Government policy and planning documents** — NDP IV, NIP 2020, MTIC Strategic Plan, Uganda Vision 2040, Tenfold Growth Strategy, sector-specific strategies and master plans.
- **Uganda Bureau of Statistics (UBOS) data** — Annual Survey of Industrial Production, National Accounts data, trade statistics, and the Census of Business Establishments.
- **Uganda Revenue Authority (URA) trade data** — import and export volumes and values by HS code, providing the basis for import substitution analysis and export market sizing.
- **Uganda Investment Authority (UIA) data** — registered investment projects by sector, indicative CAPEX, and employment projections.
- **Development partner reports** — World Bank, IFC, African Development Bank, UNIDO, and COMESA publications on Uganda's manufacturing sector and the specific value chains under review.
- **Regional and global market data** — UN Comtrade, ITC Trade Map, and industry-specific databases for market sizing, competitive benchmarking, and export opportunity identification.
- **Private sector and industry association data** — where available, production data, capacity surveys, and sector reports from associations including the Uganda Manufacturers Association (UMA), Uganda Textile Development Agency, and relevant commodity boards.

Data gaps identified during the desk review were documented and used to structure the primary data collection phase.

---

## 3.3 Key Informant Interviews

Key informant interviews (KIIs) were conducted with a purposive sample of stakeholders across each value chain. The interview sample covered five categories:

| Category | Examples |
|---|---|
| Public sector | MTIC, UIA, URA, UBOS, Uganda National Bureau of Standards (UNBS), Uganda Export Promotion Board (UEPB), relevant sector ministries |
| Private sector manufacturers | Large, medium, and small firms active in each value chain, including both domestic and foreign-owned enterprises |
| Industry associations | Uganda Manufacturers Association (UMA), commodity boards, trade associations |
| Financiers and investors | Commercial banks, development finance institutions, private equity funds active in manufacturing |
| Research and academia | Makerere University, Uganda Industrial Research Institute (UIRI), and sector-specific technical experts |

Interviews were conducted using semi-structured guides developed for each value chain. The guides were organized around the six dimensions of the analytical framework (see Section 3.5 below). All interviews were conducted under a standard confidentiality protocol; firm-level data is reported in aggregate or anonymized form throughout this report.

---

## 3.4 Field Visits

Targeted field visits were conducted to representative firms and industrial clusters within each value chain. Field visits served three purposes: verification of desk review findings against observed reality, collection of firm-level production and cost data not available from secondary sources, and assessment of operational constraints that are difficult to capture through interviews alone (energy reliability, logistics bottlenecks, standards compliance infrastructure, workforce skills levels).

Field visits were conducted at manufacturing facilities, industrial parks (including Luzira, Namanve, Buikwe, and Kampala Industrial and Business Park), and, where relevant, raw material extraction and primary processing sites.

## 3.5 Analytical Framework

Each value chain was analysed using a consistent six-part diagnostic framework. This framework was applied uniformly across all nine chains to ensure comparability of findings and recommendations.

**a. Value Chain Mapping**
The full value chain tree was mapped from primary inputs through all intermediate processing stages to final end products. This mapping identifies where Uganda currently participates in the chain, where value is lost through export of unprocessed inputs, and where import substitution opportunities exist.

**b. Current State Assessment**
Production volumes, number of active firms, employment, capacity utilization, export values, and import values were compiled for each chain. Key public and private sector players were identified. This assessment establishes the baseline from which growth trajectories and investment requirements are calculated.

**c. Binding Constraints Analysis**
Constraints were assessed across ten dimensions:

| Dimension | Key questions |
|---|---|
| Inputs and raw materials | Availability, quality, cost, and supply reliability of primary inputs |
| Technology and productivity | Vintage and condition of equipment; productivity gap relative to regional peers |
| Energy | Cost, reliability, and access to electricity and thermal energy |
| Water | Availability and cost for industrial processes |
| Logistics | Transport costs, port access, customs efficiency, cold chain availability |
| Waste and effluent management | Compliance costs, environmental liabilities, circular economy opportunities |
| Finance and investment | Access to long-term capital, interest rates, collateral requirements |
| Skills and workforce | Availability of technical, vocational, and managerial skills |
| Standards and quality | Compliance with domestic (UNBS) and international (ISO, Codex, etc.) standards |
| Policy and regulatory environment | Tariff structure, non-tariff barriers, licensing, land access, incentives regime |

**d. Market Assessment**
Markets were assessed at three levels: the domestic Ugandan market, the regional East African and COMESA market, and global export markets. For each level, the assessment covers: current market size, growth trajectory, Uganda's current market share, the competitive landscape, and Uganda's realistic market access potential given existing and achievable capabilities.

**e. Prioritization Scoring**
Based on the value chain map, current state, constraints analysis, and market assessment, each identified product or sub-sector within the chain was scored against the five prioritization criteria (see Section 3.6 below). The 3–4 highest-scoring products or sub-sectors were identified as Uganda's priorities within each chain.

**f. Priority Action Matrix**
For each value chain, a matrix of priority actions was developed, organized by timeframe: quick wins achievable within 0–12 months, policy and institutional reforms requiring 1–3 years, and investment interventions with a 3–5 year implementation horizon.

## 3.6 Prioritization Framework

A transparent, criteria-based scoring framework was applied consistently across all nine value chains to identify the 3–4 products Uganda should prioritize within each chain. The framework reflects Jerome Nuwabaasa's core analytical principle: **successful industrial economies are built around a small number of products done exceptionally well, not a broad catalogue of marginal interventions.**

Products and sub-sectors within each chain were scored against five criteria:

| Criterion | Description | Weight |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Accessible market size | Size and growth rate of the domestic, regional, and export market Uganda can realistically access | 25% |
| 2. Uganda's comparative advantage | Availability of raw material inputs, existing productive base, geographic proximity to key markets | 25% |
| 3. Feasibility | Capital requirements, technology readiness, lead time to commercial scale, management capability requirements | 20% |
| 4. Job creation and income potential | Direct and indirect employment, wage levels, linkage effects upstream and downstream | 15% |
| 5. Import substitution impact | Value of current imports that domestic production could displace, current account benefit | 15% |

Scores were assigned on a 1–5 scale for each criterion and weighted to produce a composite priority score. The scoring and detailed weightings for each value chain are documented in the Inception Report. Products scoring in the top quartile across all five criteria were designated priority products.

Critically, the framework also requires explicit deprioritization: for each chain, the study identifies which products or sub-sectors Uganda should **not** pursue at this stage, with rationale. Pursuing too many products simultaneously has been a consistent failure mode in Uganda's industrial policy history; this study is designed to produce the clarity needed for focused resource allocation.

## 3.7 Validation

Findings were validated through two mechanisms before finalization:

**Technical Working Group (TWG) review** — draft findings for each value chain were shared with the TWG, comprising MTIC officials, line ministry representatives, and development partner technical advisors. TWG feedback was incorporated into the final drafts.

**Stakeholder validation workshop** — a structured workshop was held with TWG members, private sector actors, industry associations, and development partners. The workshop tested the prioritization findings and priority action matrices against participants' knowledge and identified any material gaps or contested findings. Workshop outputs are reflected in the final chapter recommendations.

## 3.8 Limitations

This study was conducted under time and resource constraints that imposed the following limitations:

- **Data gaps at the firm level** — Uganda's industrial statistics system does not yet capture comprehensive, up-to-date production, employment, and trade data at the firm level for all nine value chains. Where primary data was unavailable, estimates were derived from proxy indicators and triangulated across multiple sources. Data gaps are flagged explicitly in each chapter with `DATA NEEDED` markers where they affect the precision of findings.
- **Market data currency** — some secondary market data, particularly for niche sub-sectors, dates from 2022 or earlier. Where data currency is a concern, this is noted in the relevant chapter.
- **Scope boundaries** — this study covers nine designated value chains. Cross-chain linkages (for example, plastics packaging serving the pharmaceuticals chain) are noted but not fully analysed, as each chain is treated as a discrete unit.
- **Validation sample** — stakeholder consultation was purposive rather than statistically representative. Findings reflect the weight of expert and practitioner judgement rather than a statistically significant survey of the sector population.

These limitations do not undermine the analytical conclusions of the study. They do indicate where further data collection and monitoring would strengthen the evidence base for implementation.
