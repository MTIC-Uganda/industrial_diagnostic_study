## 8.0 Why Plastics & Packaging Matters for the Tenfold Growth Strategy

Packaging is an enabling industry for *every other* manufacturing chain — food, beverages, sugar, fertilizer, cement and pharmaceuticals all need it. A ten-times-larger manufacturing economy needs a ten-times-larger packaging base, and it cannot be built on 100% imported resin and imported finished packaging. The tenfold play here is twofold: **substitute the packaging import bill with higher-value domestic conversion**, and **build circularity** — turning Uganda's large plastic-waste stream into domestic feedstock — so growth does not mean proportionally more pollution. The 2025 move toward a single-use-bag ban reshapes the sector toward exactly this: recycled content, durable packaging, and alternatives.

## 8.A Value Chain Map

| Phase | Activity | Main inputs | Output | Uganda's position |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| I | Resin / feedstock | Petrochemicals (imported) | PE, PP, PET, PVC resin | **Gap** — all imported (until Ch.6 park) |
| II | Conversion | Resin | Films, sheets, preforms, mouldings | **Established** |
| III | Packaging & products | Converted plastic | Bottles, sacks, containers, household goods | **Active** |
| IV | Use | Products | Consumer/industrial use | — |
| V | Recycling | Post-consumer plastic | Recycled flake/pellet | **Under-scaled** |

**Where value is captured and lost.** Uganda imports **all virgin polymer resin** (no domestic production until the petrochemical park, Chapter 6) and a large share of finished plastics. Plastic packaging (HS 3923) imports were ~**USD 37.1 million in 2024** (ITC TradeMap), and Uganda imports roughly **150,000 tonnes of plastics a year** (NEMA). Domestic value addition is in conversion; feedstock and much finished product are imported — and the waste largely uncaptured.

## 8.B Current Status

### Status snapshot

| Indicator | Value | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Plastic packaging imports (HS 3923) | ~USD 37.1m (2024) | ITC TradeMap |
| Plastics imported | ~150,000 t/yr | NEMA |
| Certified bag manufacturers | **41 firms, 67 valid UNBS permits** | UNBS |
| Plastic waste generated | **~600 t/day, <40% properly collected** | NEMA |
| Policy | National Strategy for Plastics Circularity 2023–2028; **2025 single-use-bag ban move** | NEMA |
| Leading firms | Nice House of Plastics (est. 1970, largest), Nile Plastic Industries; Quality Plastics (recycler, Mukono) | sector |

**Industry.** An established conversion industry in Kampala, Wakiso, Jinja, Mukono; **41 UNBS-certified manufacturers of plastic bags hold 67 valid permits**. Leading firms include **Nice House of Plastics** (largest, est. 1970) and **Nile Plastic Industries**; recyclers such as **Quality Plastics** run a growing but under-scaled loop.

**Waste & circularity.** ~**600 t/day** of plastic waste, **under 40% properly collected** (NEMA). The **Plastics Circularity Strategy (2023–2028)** could cut plastic pollution up to ~37% by 2040 if implemented.

**2025 policy shift.** NEMA moved to **ban single-use plastic carrier bags** (amending the National Environment Act), aligning with EAC neighbours — constraining single-use bags while creating demand for **alternatives, recycled content and durable packaging**.

## 8.C Market Analysis (four levels) and 2040 Projection

| Level | Size / Uganda position | Drivers |
|---|---|---|
| **Uganda (domestic)** | Large, growing packaging demand (food, beverage, agri-inputs, cement bags, pharma) met substantially by imports | Manufacturing growth across all chains; consumer goods |
| **Regional (EAC + COMESA)** | Accessible packaging market; EAC bag-ban alignment levels the field for compliant alternatives | Regional manufacturing/packaging demand |
| **Continental (Africa)** | Large packaging deficit; AfCFTA access | Industrialisation; consumer growth |
| **Global** | Not relevant; domestic/regional substitution + circularity | — |

### 2040 projection — Tenfold logic

Packaging demand scales with *all* manufacturing, so a tenfold economy implies a several-fold larger packaging market by 2040 — but the strategy is to meet it with **domestic conversion + recycled content + (eventually) domestic resin** from the Kabalega park (Chapter 6), not rising imports and waste. Recycling should grow from a marginal activity to a core feedstock source. Other markets grow on business-as-usual; Uganda competes **regionally** in compliant, recycled-content packaging.

## 8.D Binding Constraints — Weighted

| # | Constraint (category) | Weight | Evidence / gravity |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **100% imported virgin resin (inputs)** | **5** | No domestic resin until the petrochemical park (Ch.6); recycled feedstock under-developed — the core cost/supply constraint |
| 2 | **Low waste collection / weak circularity (waste)** | **4** | ~600 t/day waste, <40% collected — pollution liability and a missed feedstock opportunity |
| 3 | **Capital & fragmentation (finance)** | **3** | Recycling plants and modern converters need capital; sector is fragmented |
| 4 | **Policy transition risk (policy)** | **3** | The single-use-ban trajectory is opportunity for recyclers/alternatives but risk for single-use producers; clarity needed |
| 5 | **Standards & substandard bags (standards)** | **3** | UNBS certification (thickness, food-grade) central; sub-standard bags are a quality and environmental problem |
| 6 | **Logistics (infrastructure)** | **2** | Imported resin bears the landlocked penalty |
| 7 | **Skills (skills)** | **2** | Polymer-processing and recycling skills limited |

The two binding constraints — **imported resin** and **weak circularity** — point to the same solution: **build recycling** to create domestic feedstock now, and **sequence domestic resin** to the refinery later.

## 8.E Greening & Sustainability

For plastics, **circularity *is* the strategy** — environmental performance and competitiveness are the same thing here.

| Dimension | Status / opportunity |
|---|---|
| **Current status** | High waste, low collection (<40%); single-use bags being phased down |
| **Current solutions** | Circularity Strategy 2023–2028; early recyclers (Quality Plastics); UNBS standards |
| **Emerging solutions (competitiveness enablers)** | (1) **Plastics recycling (PET/HDPE flake & pellet)** turning waste into domestic feedstock; (2) **Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR)** and deposit-return to lift collection; (3) **recycled-content & durable/refillable packaging**; (4) **bio-based/compostable alternatives** to single-use items |

**Why it pays:** recycling substitutes imported resin (cost + supply security), EPR funds collection, and recycled-content/compostable products meet the 2025 policy and regional bans — converting an environmental liability into a feedstock industry.

## 8.F Prioritized Products — through the Tenfold Lens

| Product / sub-sector | Market | Comp. adv. | Feasibility | Jobs | Import subst. | Green fit | Priority |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Industrial & food/beverage packaging** | High (USD 37m+) | Medium | High | High | High | Medium | **Priority** |
| **Plastics recycling (flake/pellet)** | High (circularity) | Medium | Medium | High | High (feedstock) | High | **Priority** |
| **Agricultural & cement packaging (sacks, films)** | High | Medium | High | High | High | Medium | **Priority** |
| Single-use carrier bags | Declining (ban) | — | — | — | — | — | Deprioritize |
| Domestic virgin-resin production | High | Low now | Low | Medium | Very high | Low | Conditional (sequence to refinery, Ch.6) |

**The three priorities for Plastics & Packaging:**

1. **Industrial and food/beverage packaging** — substitute the USD 37m+ packaging imports with higher-value, standards-compliant domestic packaging for food, beverage and agri-input sectors.
2. **Plastics recycling** — scale recycling to turn the 600 t/day waste stream into domestic feedstock; the strongest alignment with the 2025 circularity policy.
3. **Agricultural and cement packaging** (woven sacks, films) — feasible, job-rich, and linked to Uganda's agriculture and cement sectors.

**Explicit deprioritization.** Uganda should **not** expand **single-use carrier-bag** capacity (stranded-asset risk under the 2025 policy). Domestic **virgin-resin** is conditional and must be **sequenced to the petrochemical park** (Chapter 6).

## 8.G Project Profiles

**P1 — Plastics recycling capacity (green + strategic anchor).**
- *Strategic:* turns the 600 t/day waste stream into domestic feedstock; cuts resin imports; aligns with circularity policy.
- *Quick-win element:* build on existing recyclers (Quality Plastics) + EPR collection.
- *Green:* the flagship circular-economy project.

**P2 — Modern food/beverage & agri-packaging plant (strategic).**
- *Strategic:* substitutes USD 37m+ packaging imports; serves every other chain.
- *Quick-win element:* expand certified converters; standards-compliant lines.
- *Green:* recycled-content packaging.

**P3 — EPR & collection scheme (green + quick-ish).**
- *Strategic:* lifts collection above 40%, feeding recyclers.
- *Quick-win element:* policy + producer-funded collection can start near-term.
- *Green:* directly cuts pollution.
