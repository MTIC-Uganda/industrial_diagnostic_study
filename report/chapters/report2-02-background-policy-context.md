## 2.1 Uganda's Industrialization Ambition

Uganda has set out an ambitious long-term industrialization trajectory anchored in a hierarchy of national planning instruments. These instruments collectively define the targets, sectoral priorities, and institutional responsibilities that frame this diagnostic study.

**Uganda Vision 2040** sets the overarching national direction: transformation from a predominantly peasant and low-income economy to a competitive upper-middle-income country within thirty years. The Vision identifies industrialization — particularly agro-processing and resource-based manufacturing — as the central engine of this transformation. It sets a target of increasing the manufacturing sector's contribution to GDP to at least 20%, and projects Uganda's per capita income rising to USD 9,500 by 2040.

**The National Development Plan IV (NDP IV), 2020/21–2024/25** operationalizes Vision 2040 over a five-year cycle. NDP IV designates manufacturing as one of three core growth areas alongside agriculture and services. Its industrialization targets are specific: raise manufacturing's share of GDP to 21%, increase manufactured exports to 35% of total exports, and reduce Uganda's import bill through deliberate import substitution in priority value chains. NDP IV identifies Textiles & Garments, Pharmaceuticals, Petrochemicals & Fertilizers, Sugar & Confectionery, Plastics & Packaging, and Cement & Building Materials as priority chains for industrial policy intervention, anchored in Uganda's agricultural raw material base, growing domestic market, and regional trade opportunities.

**The National Industrial Policy (NIP) 2020** provides the sector-specific framework within which NDP IV industrial targets are pursued. The NIP establishes Uganda's industrial policy objectives as: building productive capacity in selected manufacturing value chains, enhancing domestic value addition, reducing dependence on imported intermediate and capital goods, and linking industrial development to Uganda's natural resource base. The NIP designates MTIC as the lead coordinating ministry for industrial policy implementation and calls for targeted diagnostics of priority value chains as the analytical foundation for investment decisions and policy reforms.

**The MTIC Strategic Plan 2020/21–2024/25** translates NIP objectives into ministerial priorities. MTIC's strategic focus includes: facilitating private sector investment in priority industries, leading technical working groups for value chain development, coordinating with Uganda Investment Authority (UIA), Uganda Export Promotion Board (UEPB), Uganda National Bureau of Standards (UNBS), and other agencies, and mobilizing development partner support for industrial development. This diagnostic study is a direct output of MTIC's strategic mandate.

**The Tenfold Growth Strategy** represents Uganda's most ambitious industrial growth target. It sets a goal of growing Uganda's economy tenfold over twenty years, driven primarily by manufacturing and exports. Under the Tenfold Strategy, manufacturing must grow at 12–15% annually — approximately double the historical average. Achieving this requires structural transformation: moving from low-value raw material and commodity exports to processed and manufactured goods that capture a larger share of global and regional value chains. The six value chains in this report — spanning agro-processing, chemicals, and construction materials — are central to this transformation.

## 2.2 The Uganda–UNIDO Programme for Country Partnership

The Uganda–UNIDO Programme for Country Partnership (PCP) for Inclusive and Sustainable Industrial Development (ISID) is the multilateral cooperation framework within which this diagnostic study is produced. The PCP is a government-led partnership model developed by UNIDO to mobilize coordinated technical assistance and investment around a country's industrial development priorities.

The Uganda PCP operates across six priority areas:

1. **Strengthening industrial policy implementation and institutional coordination** — supporting MTIC and line agencies to design, implement, and monitor industrial policy effectively.

2. **Mobilizing large-scale public and private investment for agro- and mineral-based industrialization** — developing the investment pipeline in priority value chains and creating conditions for private capital mobilization.

3. **Supporting sustainable industrial parks and free zones** — developing infrastructure that clusters manufacturing activity, reduces per-firm costs, and attracts foreign direct investment.

4. **Strengthening national standards, quality infrastructure, and accreditation systems** — building Uganda's capacity to meet international standards requirements, a binding constraint to export competitiveness particularly for pharmaceuticals, food products, and textiles.

5. **Advancing green industry, circular economy, and Industry 4.0 solutions** — embedding environmental sustainability and technology upgrading into Uganda's industrial development model.

6. **Supporting productive use of renewable energy and energy efficiency** — reducing energy costs for manufacturers, which remain among the highest in East Africa and a significant competitiveness constraint.

This diagnostic study directly supports PCP Priority Areas 1 and 2. Its findings will serve as technical inputs into PCP formulation, partner coordination, and investment mobilization specifically for the six value chains covered in this report.

## 2.3 Current State of Uganda's Manufacturing Sector

Uganda's manufacturing sector operates well below its potential. The sector currently contributes approximately **16% of GDP**, against an NDP IV target of 21% and a Vision 2040 target of 20%+. This gap reflects structural weaknesses that have persisted across successive planning cycles.

**Capacity utilization** across the manufacturing sector averages approximately **30%**. This is one of the lowest utilization rates in East Africa and reflects the combined effect of unreliable and expensive energy, weak demand, input supply constraints, skills mismatches, and limited access to finance. Low utilization means manufacturers bear high fixed costs per unit of output, making them uncompetitive against imports and unable to generate the cash flows needed for reinvestment.

Key structural characteristics of Uganda's manufacturing base relevant to this report include:

- **Dominance of agro-processing and light manufacturing** — the sector is concentrated in food and beverages, textiles, and building materials. These are precisely the value chains covered in Report 2, indicating that Uganda has an existing productive base to build on, not a greenfield challenge.
- **Underutilized agricultural raw materials** — Uganda produces cotton, sugarcane, and other agricultural commodities that are exported with minimal processing. The Report 2 value chains represent the processing layers that should be capturing this value domestically.
- **High pharmaceutical and chemical import dependency** — Uganda imports the bulk of its medicines, agrochemicals, fertilizers, and plastic packaging. This creates chronic current account pressure and represents a large and addressable opportunity for domestic import substitution.
- **Growing construction and housing demand** — rapid urbanization and infrastructure investment are driving demand for cement and building materials, a sector where Uganda has an existing production base and limestone resources.

Despite these constraints, Uganda's manufacturing sector has genuine competitive foundations for the Report 2 value chains: abundant cotton, sugarcane, and other agricultural raw material inputs; a large and growing domestic market; proximity to DRC and South Sudan as major consumer markets; and improving logistics infrastructure.

## 2.4 Rationale for the Six Priority Value Chains in This Report

The six value chains covered in this report — Textiles & Garments, Pharmaceuticals, Petrochemicals & Fertilizers, Sugar & Confectionery, Plastics & Packaging, and Cement & Building Materials — were selected by MTIC through a deliberate prioritization process drawing on NDP IV designations, NIP sectoral priorities, Uganda's raw material base, import substitution potential, and regional market structure.

**Textiles & Garments** was selected on the basis of Uganda's cotton production base (Uganda grows high-quality long-staple cotton), the existing but underperforming textile manufacturing industry, and the large regional and global market for garments. Uganda has the agricultural foundation to supply a domestic textile industry; the challenge is closing the gap between cotton farming and competitive garment manufacturing. Regional and international trade agreements (EAC, AGOA) provide preferential market access that Uganda has not yet fully exploited.

**Pharmaceuticals** was selected because Uganda imports approximately 80% of its medicines by value, despite having an existing domestic pharmaceutical manufacturing base of over 30 registered manufacturers. The COVID-19 pandemic exposed the strategic vulnerability of import dependency in medicines. Regional demand — particularly from DRC and South Sudan — is large and growing. Strengthening domestic pharmaceutical manufacturing is both a public health imperative and a significant economic opportunity.

**Petrochemicals & Fertilizers** was selected in the context of Uganda's emerging oil and gas sector. First oil production is anticipated within the next few years, and associated gas and petroleum feedstocks present an opportunity to develop downstream petrochemical processing. Uganda also has a critical fertilizer deficit: low soil fertility and limited fertilizer use are among the most important constraints on agricultural productivity. Domestic fertilizer production — particularly blended fertilizers matched to Ugandan soil profiles — represents a high-impact import substitution opportunity.

**Sugar & Confectionery** was selected because Uganda is one of East Africa's largest sugar producers, with significant installed capacity. However, Uganda's sugar industry operates below potential, and the value chain beyond raw sugar — confectionery, industrial sugar products, and ethanol — is underdeveloped. Regional sugar demand is growing, and Uganda's central EAC location provides a natural export advantage for processed sugar products.

**Plastics & Packaging** was selected because plastic packaging is a critical input across virtually all manufacturing sectors — food processing, pharmaceuticals, consumer goods. Uganda currently imports significant volumes of plastic packaging that could be manufactured domestically. The value chain is also increasingly important for the circular economy, with recycled plastics presenting a growing opportunity.

**Cement & Building Materials** was selected because Uganda has limestone deposits and an existing cement manufacturing base, but still imports significant volumes of cement and building materials to meet construction demand. Regional demand from DRC, South Sudan, and Rwanda — all of which are major construction markets with limited domestic cement capacity — presents an export opportunity that Uganda is positioned to capture.

The analytical question across all six chains is the same: given Uganda's resource base, competitive position, and institutional capacity, **which 3–4 products or sub-sectors within each chain offer the most credible path to competitive manufacturing at scale?** This report provides that prioritization.

## 2.5 NDP IV and Tenfold Targets Relevant to This Report

The following specific targets from NDP IV and the Tenfold Growth Strategy frame the ambition against which this report's recommendations are calibrated:

| Indicator | Current Baseline | NDP IV Target (2024/25) | Tenfold Target (2040) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Manufacturing share of GDP | 16% | 21% | 20%+ |
| Manufactured exports as % of total exports | ~12% | 35% | >50% |
| Capacity utilization (manufacturing) | ~30% | 60%+ | 75%+ |
| Annual manufacturing sector growth rate | ~6% | 12% | 12–15% |
| Pharmaceutical import dependency | ~80% of medicines by value | Significantly reduced | <40% |
| Fertilizer use (kg/hectare) | Among lowest in Africa | Increasing | Competitive with EAC peers |
| Cotton lint processed domestically | <20% | >50% | >80% |

Achieving these targets in the Report 2 value chains requires both deepening existing productive capacity and making targeted new investments where Uganda has a clear competitive foundation. The diagnostic findings and recommendations in Chapters 4 through 9 are calibrated to this ambition.
