## 2.1 Uganda's Industrialization Ambition

Uganda's long-term industrialization trajectory is anchored in a hierarchy of national planning instruments that define the targets, sectoral priorities, and institutional responsibilities framing this diagnostic.

**Uganda Vision 2040** sets the overarching direction: transformation to a competitive upper-middle-income country, targeting a GDP of approximately USD 581 billion and per-capita income of USD 9,500 by 2040, with industrialization — particularly agro-processing and resource-based manufacturing — as a central engine (National Planning Authority, *Uganda Vision 2040*).

**The Fourth National Development Plan (NDP IV), FY2025/26–2029/30**, approved by Parliament in January 2026 under the theme *"Sustainable Industrialization for inclusive growth, employment, and wealth creation,"* targets GDP of USD 158 billion by FY2029/30 (from USD 53.2 billion in FY2023/24), per-capita income of USD 2,008, and 10.1% annual growth by FY2029/30 (Parliament of Uganda; Ministry of Finance, Planning and Economic Development). The six value chains in this report — Textiles & Garments, Pharmaceuticals, Petrochemicals & Fertilizers, Sugar & Confectionery, Plastics & Packaging, and Cement & Building Materials — are among the priority manufacturing chains identified for industrial-policy support, anchored largely in Uganda's agricultural base and its import-substitution opportunities.

**The National Industrial Policy (NIP) 2020** provides the sector framework: raising manufacturing's contribution to GDP, deepening domestic value addition, and reducing dependence on imported intermediate and finished goods (MTIC, *NIP 2020*). It designates MTIC as lead coordinating ministry and calls for value-chain diagnostics — the mandate for this study.

**The MTIC Strategic Plan** translates these objectives into ministerial priorities: facilitating private investment, leading value-chain technical working groups, and coordinating Uganda Investment Authority (UIA), Uganda Export Promotion Board (UEPB), UNBS, NDA, NEMA, and other agencies.

**The Tenfold Growth Strategy** targets growing the economy roughly tenfold (≈USD 50bn → USD 500bn), organized around **ATMs — Agro-industrialization, Tourism, Minerals, and Science, Technology & Innovation (STI)**. For Report 2, **agro-industrialization** is the dominant driver: textiles (cotton), sugar, and pharmaceuticals all build on Uganda's agricultural and resource base, while cement, plastics, and fertilizers are core import-substitution and input industries.

## 2.2 The Uganda–UNIDO Programme for Country Partnership

The Uganda–UNIDO Programme for Country Partnership (PCP) for Inclusive and Sustainable Industrial Development (ISID) is the framework within which this study is produced — a government-led model to mobilize coordinated technical assistance and investment around Uganda's industrial priorities (UNIDO, *Programme for Country Partnership*).

The Uganda PCP operates across six priority areas:

1. **Strengthening industrial policy implementation and institutional coordination.**
2. **Mobilizing large-scale public and private investment for agro- and mineral-based industrialization.**
3. **Supporting sustainable industrial parks and free zones.**
4. **Strengthening national standards, quality infrastructure, and accreditation systems** — especially important for pharmaceuticals (NDA/WHO-GMP) and food/cement standards.
5. **Advancing green industry, circular economy, and Industry 4.0 solutions** — directly relevant to plastics circularity.
6. **Supporting productive use of renewable energy and energy efficiency** — relevant to energy-intensive cement.

This study supports PCP Priority Areas 1 and 2, providing technical inputs for the six Report 2 value chains.

## 2.3 Current State of Uganda's Manufacturing Sector

Manufacturing value added was about **15.2% of GDP in 2024** (World Bank, WDI), below the NIP 2020 target of ~16% by FY2029/30, at roughly 30% capacity utilization (indicative; to be confirmed from latest UBOS data).

> **DATA NOTE:** The "~30% capacity utilization" figure should be confirmed against the latest Uganda Bureau of Statistics (UBOS) data before finalization; it is used here as indicative and attributed accordingly.

Structural characteristics relevant to Report 2:

- **Agro-processing under-capture** — Uganda exports raw agricultural commodities (notably cotton lint) and imports the processed product (textiles, garments), forgoing value addition (ITC TradeMap, 2024).
- **High import dependence in manufactured essentials** — pharmaceuticals, fertilizers, plastics, and cement clinker are substantially imported, creating both current-account pressure and import-substitution opportunity.
- **Established processing in sugar and cement** — unlike Report 1's metals, sugar and cement are areas where Uganda already has competitive domestic production and, in sugar, structural surplus.
- **Strong regional export orientation** — over 70% of Uganda's manufactured exports go to regional markets (Uganda Investment Authority, 2025).

## 2.4 Rationale for the Six Priority Value Chains in This Report

The six chains were selected through MTIC's prioritization process drawing on NDP IV, NIP priorities, Uganda's agricultural and resource base, and import-substitution potential.

**Textiles & Garments** — Uganda grows cotton but exports ~90% of its lint raw at under USD 1/kg, with only two vertically integrated mills consuming the rest (Cotton Development Organisation; EPRC). The value-addition gap from lint to fabric to garment is among the largest in the economy.

**Pharmaceuticals** — Uganda imports ~90% of its medicines (down from 96% in 2016); the medical and pharmaceutical import bill reached **USD 534 million in 2023**, with a domestic market around USD 221 million and a small but growing WHO-GMP manufacturing base led by Cipla QCIL (Statista; UNIDO Pharmaceutical Sector Profile; NDA).

**Petrochemicals & Fertilizers** — Uganda's emerging oil sector (the Kabaale refinery and Kabalega Petrochemical Industrial Park) creates a future petrochemical feedstock base, while fertilizer demand vastly exceeds the stalled domestic supply at Sukulu — a critical agricultural-input gap (UNOC; PAU; Osukuru complex records).

**Sugar & Confectionery** — Uganda is in structural sugar surplus (~822,000 t output vs ~370,000 t consumption), making downstream diversification (industrial sugar, confectionery, ethanol, co-generation) the priority rather than more raw sugar (Uganda Sugar Manufacturers' Association; sector data).

**Plastics & Packaging** — Uganda imports ~150,000 t of plastics a year and is a net importer of packaging, while facing a 2025 policy shift toward a single-use-plastic ban and circularity (NEMA).

**Cement & Building Materials** — a domestic-production success with a market of **USD 707 million (2024)**, but dependent on imported clinker (>50%) — the clear value-addition and import-substitution target (IndexBox; CemNet).

The analytical question is identical across all six: **which 3–4 products or sub-sectors offer the most credible path to competitive manufacturing at scale?**

## 2.5 NDP IV and Tenfold Targets Relevant to This Report

| Indicator | Current Baseline | NDP IV / NIP direction | Tenfold horizon (2040) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Manufacturing share of GDP | 15.2% (2024, World Bank) | toward 16%+ by FY29/30 (NIP) | 20%+ (Vision 2040) |
| GDP | USD 53.2bn (FY23/24) | USD 158bn by FY29/30 (NDP IV) | ~USD 500bn (Tenfold) |
| Per capita income | USD 1,146 (FY23/24) | USD 2,008 by FY29/30 (NDP IV) | USD 9,500 (Vision 2040) |
| Manufactured exports to region | >70% regional (UIA 2025) | rising | dominant |
| Cotton lint value addition | ~10% processed domestically | rising | majority processed |
| Pharmaceutical local production | ~10% of market | rising | substantial |

Achieving these targets requires moving from raw-commodity export and finished-goods import toward domestic value addition. Chapters 4–9 provide the chain-by-chain prioritization.
