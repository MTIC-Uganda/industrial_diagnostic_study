## 4.A Value Chain Map

The textiles and garments chain runs from cotton cultivation through ginning, spinning, weaving/knitting, and finishing to garment manufacture. Uganda participates strongly at the **raw end (cotton growing and ginning)** and the **finished end (limited integrated mills)**, but exports most of its lint raw — the defining value-leak of the chain.

| Phase | Activity | Main inputs | Output |
|---|---|---|---|
| I | Cotton growing | Seed, inputs | Seed cotton |
| II | Ginning | Seed cotton | Lint + cottonseed |
| III | Spinning | Lint | Yarn |
| IV | Weaving / knitting | Yarn | Fabric |
| V | Wet processing & finishing | Fabric | Dyed/finished fabric |
| VI | Garment manufacture | Fabric | Garments, apparel, home textiles |

**Where value is captured and lost.** Uganda exports roughly **90% of its cotton lint raw at under USD 1/kg** — exporting about **USD 11.3 million of cotton (HS 5201) in 2024** (ITC TradeMap) — while only two vertically integrated mills consume the remaining ~10% domestically (CDO; EPRC). The country then imports finished textiles and garments. The opportunity is to move lint into domestic yarn, fabric, and garments.

---

## 4.B Current State Assessment

**Cotton production.** Uganda produced about **116,000 bales in 2023** (≈200 kg lint each), with ~130,000 bales anticipated for 2024, against a good-year potential of up to 250,000 bales (CDO; sector reporting). There are **39 ginneries with installed capacity near one million bales/year — operating at only ~10% of capacity** (CDO), a striking measure of under-utilization.

**Mills and value addition.** Uganda has **two vertically integrated mills**: **Southern Range Nyanza (Nytil)** and **Fine Spinners Ltd**, together consuming ~10% of national lint and generating around **USD 50 million annual turnover**; Fine Spinners alone is associated with ~18,000 jobs across its operations and outgrower base (ChimpReports; UIA). New investment includes the Lira spinning facility under the cotton-development programme.

**Trade position (ITC TradeMap, 2024).** Cotton lint exports ~USD 11.3m; imports of knitted T-shirts (HS 6109) ~USD 6.7m, alongside broader apparel and fabric imports. Uganda is a **net exporter of raw lint and a net importer of finished textiles/garments** — the inverse of a value-adding industry.

## 4.C Binding Constraints Analysis

| Dimension | Assessment |
|---|---|
| **Inputs / raw materials** | Cotton output is volatile and well below capacity; seed quality and agronomy limit yields. Lint is abundant relative to domestic processing. |
| **Technology & productivity** | Spinning/weaving capacity is small; much equipment is dated; wet-processing (dyeing/finishing) is a particular gap. |
| **Energy** | Spinning and wet-processing are energy- and water-intensive; tariff/reliability matter. |
| **Water** | Wet-processing needs reliable, treatable water; effluent compliance (NEMA) is a constraint and cost. |
| **Logistics** | Landlocked costs (~18–20% of sale price) erode competitiveness against Asian textile exporters. |
| **Waste & effluent** | Dyeing effluent regulated under NEMA 2020; treatment capacity needed for scale-up. |
| **Finance & investment** | Spinning/weaving mills are capital-intensive; long-term finance scarce. |
| **Skills & workforce** | Textile-engineering and garment-supervisory skills limited; garment work is trainable and job-rich. |
| **Standards & quality** | Standards and consistency needed to access export and regional markets. |
| **Policy & regulatory** | EAC used-clothing (mivumba) policy, cotton-sector regulation (CDO), and incentives shape the chain. Used-clothing imports compete directly with domestic garments. |

## 4.D Market Assessment

**Domestic.** Growing demand for apparel, uniforms, and home textiles, currently met substantially by imports and used clothing. Institutional demand (uniforms for schools, security, health) is an addressable anchor.

**Regional (EAC/COMESA).** Regional apparel and fabric markets are accessible; Uganda's cotton base is a comparative advantage if processed. AGOA/EU access has historically supported garment export, subject to policy continuity.

**Global.** Commodity textile export is highly competitive (Asia dominates); Uganda's realistic global niche is **organic/traceable cotton and specialty apparel**, not commodity volume.

**Accessible market conclusion.** Prioritize **domestic and regional garments/home textiles** and **yarn/fabric for those markets**, anchored on Uganda's own cotton; plus a **premium organic-cotton** export niche.

## 4.E Prioritized Products

| Product / sub-sector | Market | Comp. adv. | Feasibility | Jobs | Import subst. | Priority |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Garments & apparel (incl. institutional uniforms)** | High | Medium | High | Very high | High | **Priority** |
| **Yarn & fabric (spinning/weaving)** | High | High (lint) | Medium | High | High | **Priority** |
| **Cottonseed oil & cake (by-products)** | Medium | High | High | Medium | Medium | **Priority** |
| Organic/traceable cotton (premium export) | Medium | High | Medium | Medium | n/a | Conditional |
| Commodity textile export to global market | Low | Low | Low | Medium | Low | Deprioritize |

**The three-to-four priorities for Textiles & Garments:**

1. **Garments & apparel** — the most job-rich step, anchored by institutional/uniform demand and regional markets; converts fabric into the highest-value, highest-employment product.
2. **Yarn & fabric (spinning/weaving)** — process domestic lint instead of exporting it raw; the core value-addition move. Build on Nytil, Fine Spinners, and the Lira facility.
3. **Cottonseed oil & cake** — capture ginning by-products (edible oil, animal feed), a feasible quick value-add.

**Explicit deprioritization.** Uganda should **not** chase commodity textile export to global markets against Asian scale. Focus on **domestic/regional garments and fabric from Ugandan cotton**, with organic cotton as a premium niche.
