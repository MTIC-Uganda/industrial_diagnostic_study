## 4.0 Why Textiles & Garments Matters for the Tenfold Growth Strategy

Textiles is the flagship of **agro-industrialisation** — the "A" in the Tenfold Growth Strategy's ATMs. It turns a crop Uganda already grows (cotton) into the most job-rich manufacturing there is: ginning → spinning → weaving → garments. Today Uganda does the opposite of value addition — it **exports ~90% of its cotton lint raw at under USD 1/kg** and re-imports finished cloth and clothing. For a ten-times-larger economy that must create mass employment, capturing the spinning-to-garment value on Uganda's own cotton is among the highest-return moves available: every step up the chain multiplies jobs and value per kilogram of lint.

## 4.A Value Chain Map

| Phase | Activity | Main inputs | Output | Uganda's position |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| I | Cotton growing | Seed, inputs | Seed cotton | **Strong** (smallholder base) |
| II | Ginning | Seed cotton | Lint + cottonseed | **Strong** (39 ginneries) |
| III | Spinning | Lint | Yarn | **Weak** — limited |
| IV | Weaving / knitting | Yarn | Fabric | **Weak** |
| V | Wet processing & finishing | Fabric | Dyed/finished fabric | **Gap** — key bottleneck |
| VI | Garment manufacture | Fabric | Garments, apparel, home textiles | **Limited** |

**Where value is captured and lost.** Uganda exports roughly **90% of its cotton lint raw at under USD 1/kg** — about **USD 11.3 million of cotton (HS 5201) in 2024** (ITC TradeMap) — while only two vertically integrated mills consume the remaining ~10% domestically (CDO; EPRC). It then imports finished textiles and garments (e.g. ~USD 6.7m of knitted T-shirts, HS 6109). The opportunity is to move lint into domestic yarn, fabric and garments.

## 4.B Current Status

### Status snapshot

| Indicator | Value | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Cotton output | ~116,000 bales (2023); ~130,000 expected 2024; up to ~250,000 in a good year | CDO |
| Ginneries | **39, ~1 million bales/yr capacity — operating at only ~10%** | CDO |
| Integrated mills | **2** (Nytil; Fine Spinners) — ~10% of lint; ~USD 50m turnover | CDO; UIA |
| Employment (Fine Spinners + outgrowers) | ~18,000 | ChimpReports; UIA |
| Lint exports | ~USD 11.3m (HS 5201, 2024) | ITC TradeMap |
| Garment imports (indicative) | ~USD 6.7m knitted T-shirts (HS 6109) + broader apparel | ITC TradeMap |

**Cotton production.** ~**116,000 bales in 2023** (≈200 kg lint each), ~130,000 expected 2024, against good-year potential up to 250,000 (CDO). **39 ginneries** hold capacity near one million bales/year but run at only **~10%** — a striking under-utilisation.

**Mills and value addition.** Two vertically integrated mills — **Southern Range Nyanza (Nytil)** and **Fine Spinners Ltd** — consume ~10% of national lint and generate ~**USD 50m turnover**; Fine Spinners alone is associated with ~18,000 jobs across operations and outgrowers (UIA). New investment includes the **Lira spinning facility** under the cotton-development programme.

**Trade position.** Net **exporter of raw lint** and net **importer of finished textiles/garments** — the inverse of a value-adding industry.

## 4.C Market Analysis (four levels) and 2040 Projection

| Level | Size / Uganda position | Drivers |
|---|---|---|
| **Uganda (domestic)** | Apparel/uniforms/home-textiles demand met largely by imports + used clothing; institutional demand addressable | Population, incomes, institutional procurement |
| **Regional (EAC + COMESA)** | Accessible apparel/fabric markets; Uganda's cotton base is an advantage if processed | Regional demand; EAC integration |
| **Continental (Africa)** | Large apparel market; AGOA/AfCFTA access for garments | Africa's young population; preferential access |
| **Global** | Asia-dominated commodity textiles; Uganda's niche is **organic/traceable cotton & specialty apparel** | Sustainability-driven sourcing |

### 2040 projection — Tenfold logic

A ten-times-larger, more urban, higher-income Uganda implies a **multi-fold increase in apparel and home-textile demand**, and the chance to convert today's ~1m-bale ginning capacity (only 10% used) into a full spinning-to-garment industry. If Uganda processes even half its lint domestically and supplies regional markets, the textiles-and-garments sector could become one of the largest manufacturing employers by 2040. Other markets grow on business-as-usual; Uganda's external plays are **regional garments/fabric** and **premium organic cotton** globally — not commodity volume.

## 4.D Binding Constraints — Weighted

| # | Constraint (category) | Weight | Evidence / gravity |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Wet-processing/finishing gap (technology/inputs)** | **5** | Dyeing/finishing is the key missing midstream; without it, fabric and garments can't scale |
| 2 | **Used-clothing (mivumba) competition (policy)** | **4** | Used-clothing imports directly undercut domestic garments; EAC policy is decisive |
| 3 | **Cotton output volatility & yields (inputs)** | **4** | Output well below the ~1m-bale capacity (only ~10% used); seed quality/agronomy limit yields |
| 4 | **Capital for spinning/weaving (finance)** | **4** | Mills are capital-intensive; long-term finance scarce and costly |
| 5 | **Water & effluent for wet-processing (water/waste)** | **3** | Dyeing needs reliable, treatable water; NEMA effluent compliance is a cost and constraint |
| 6 | **Skills (skills)** | **3** | Textile-engineering/supervisory skills limited; garment work is trainable and job-rich |
| 7 | **Logistics (infrastructure)** | **3** | Landlocked costs (~18–20% of sale price) vs. Asian exporters |
| 8 | **Standards & consistency (standards)** | **2** | Quality consistency needed for export/regional markets |

The decisive lever is building **wet-processing/finishing** capacity (so domestic lint can become finished fabric and garments) while curbing used-clothing competition and raising cotton yields to fill idle ginning capacity.

## 4.E Greening & Sustainability

Sustainability is a **market-access advantage** in textiles: global and regional buyers increasingly demand traceable, low-impact cotton and clean wet-processing.

| Dimension | Status / opportunity |
|---|---|
| **Current status** | Largely rain-fed smallholder cotton (naturally low-input); wet-processing effluent is the main impact point |
| **Current solutions** | NEMA effluent regulation; outgrower models; cottonseed by-product use (oil, cake) |
| **Emerging solutions (competitiveness enablers)** | (1) **Organic / traceable cotton** for premium export; (2) **closed-loop water recycling & low-water dyeing** in wet-processing; (3) **solar-powered spinning/garment parks**; (4) **textile-waste recycling** and cottonseed-oil/cake circularity |

**Why it pays:** organic, traceable, clean-processed Ugandan textiles command premiums and pass the sustainability screens of EU/AGOA and regional institutional buyers — turning environmental performance into orders.

## 4.F Prioritized Products — through the Tenfold Lens

| Product / sub-sector | Market | Comp. adv. | Feasibility | Jobs | Import subst. | Green fit | Priority |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Garments & apparel (incl. institutional uniforms)** | High | Medium | High | Very high | High | Medium–High | **Priority** |
| **Yarn & fabric (spinning/weaving + finishing)** | High | High (lint) | Medium | High | High | Medium | **Priority** |
| **Cottonseed oil & cake (by-products)** | Medium | High | High | Medium | Medium | High | **Priority** |
| Organic/traceable cotton (premium export) | Medium | High | Medium | Medium | n/a | High | Conditional |
| Commodity textile export to global market | Low | Low | Low | Medium | Low | — | Deprioritize |

**The three-to-four priorities for Textiles & Garments:**

1. **Garments & apparel** — the most job-rich step; anchor with institutional/uniform demand and regional markets.
2. **Yarn & fabric (spinning/weaving + wet-processing)** — process domestic lint instead of exporting it raw; build on Nytil, Fine Spinners and the Lira facility, adding the missing finishing step.
3. **Cottonseed oil & cake** — capture ginning by-products (edible oil, animal feed); a feasible, green quick value-add.

**Explicit deprioritization.** Uganda should **not** chase commodity textile export against Asian scale. Focus on **domestic/regional garments and fabric from Ugandan cotton**, with **organic cotton** as a premium niche.

## 4.G Project Profiles

**P1 — Integrated spinning–weaving–finishing capacity (strategic anchor).**
- *Strategic:* closes the wet-processing gap so domestic lint becomes finished fabric; foundation for garments at scale.
- *Quick-win element:* expand existing mills (Nytil, Fine Spinners, Lira) rather than greenfield.
- *Green:* solar power + closed-loop water/effluent.

**P2 — Institutional-garment & uniform programme (quick win).**
- *Strategic:* guaranteed demand (schools, health, security, MDAs) anchors garment employment.
- *Quick-win element:* procurement policy can start immediately.
- *Green:* uses domestic, traceable cotton.

**P3 — Organic/traceable cotton + cottonseed processing (green + strategic).**
- *Strategic:* premium export niche + by-product value.
- *Quick-win element:* certify existing low-input cotton; install cottonseed crushing.
- *Green:* organic certification; circular by-product use.
