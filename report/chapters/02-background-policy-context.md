## 2.1 Uganda's Industrialization Ambition

Uganda has set out an ambitious long-term industrialization trajectory anchored in a hierarchy of national planning instruments. These instruments collectively define the targets, sectoral priorities, and institutional responsibilities that frame this diagnostic study.

**Uganda Vision 2040** sets the overarching national direction: transformation from a predominantly peasant and low-income economy to a competitive upper-middle-income country within thirty years. The Vision identifies industrialization — particularly agro-processing and mineral-based manufacturing — as the central engine of this transformation. It sets a target of increasing the manufacturing sector's contribution to GDP from its current level to at least 20%, and projects Uganda's per capita income rising to USD 9,500 by 2040.

**The National Development Plan IV (NDP IV), 2020/21–2024/25** operationalizes Vision 2040 over a five-year cycle. NDP IV designates manufacturing as one of three core growth areas alongside agriculture and services. Its industrialization targets are specific: raise manufacturing's share of GDP to 21%, increase manufactured exports to 35% of total exports, and reduce Uganda's import bill through deliberate import substitution in priority value chains. NDP IV identifies the nine value chains covered in this study — Iron & Steel, Copper & Allied Metals, Automotives, Textiles & Garments, Pharmaceuticals, Petrochemicals & Fertilizers, Sugar & Confectionery, Plastics & Packaging, and Cement & Building Materials — as priority chains for industrial policy intervention.

**The National Industrial Policy (NIP) 2020** provides the sector-specific framework within which NDP IV industrial targets are pursued. The NIP establishes Uganda's industrial policy objectives as: building productive capacity in selected manufacturing value chains, enhancing domestic value addition, reducing dependence on imported intermediate and capital goods, and linking industrial development to Uganda's natural resource base. The NIP designates MTIC as the lead coordinating ministry for industrial policy implementation and calls for targeted diagnostics of priority value chains as the analytical foundation for investment decisions and policy reforms.

**The MTIC Strategic Plan 2020/21–2024/25** translates NIP objectives into ministerial priorities. MTIC's strategic focus includes: facilitating private sector investment in priority industries, leading technical working groups for value chain development, coordinating with Uganda Investment Authority (UIA), Uganda Export Promotion Board (UEPB), Uganda National Bureau of Standards (UNBS), and other agencies, and mobilizing development partner support for industrial development. This diagnostic study is a direct output of MTIC's strategic mandate.

**The Tenfold Growth Strategy** represents Uganda's most ambitious industrial growth target. It sets a goal of growing Uganda's economy tenfold over twenty years, driven primarily by manufacturing and exports. Under the Tenfold Strategy, manufacturing must grow at 12–15% annually — approximately double the historical average. Achieving this requires not incremental improvements but structural transformation: moving from low-value raw material exports to processed and manufactured goods that capture a larger share of global value chains.

## 2.2 The Uganda–UNIDO Programme for Country Partnership

The Uganda–UNIDO Programme for Country Partnership (PCP) for Inclusive and Sustainable Industrial Development (ISID) is the multilateral cooperation framework within which this diagnostic study is produced. The PCP is a government-led partnership model developed by UNIDO to mobilize coordinated technical assistance and investment around a country's industrial development priorities.

The Uganda PCP operates across six priority areas:

1. **Strengthening industrial policy implementation and institutional coordination** — supporting MTIC and line agencies to design, implement, and monitor industrial policy effectively.

2. **Mobilizing large-scale public and private investment for agro- and mineral-based industrialization** — developing the investment pipeline in priority value chains and creating conditions for private capital mobilization.

3. **Supporting sustainable industrial parks and free zones** — developing infrastructure that clusters manufacturing activity, reduces per-firm costs, and attracts foreign direct investment.

4. **Strengthening national standards, quality infrastructure, and accreditation systems** — building Uganda's capacity to meet international standards requirements, a binding constraint to export competitiveness.

5. **Advancing green industry, circular economy, and Industry 4.0 solutions** — embedding environmental sustainability and technology upgrading into Uganda's industrial development model.

6. **Supporting productive use of renewable energy and energy efficiency** — reducing energy costs for manufacturers, which remain among the highest in East Africa and a significant competitiveness constraint.

This diagnostic study directly supports PCP Priority Areas 1 and 2. Its findings will serve as technical inputs into PCP formulation, partner coordination, and investment mobilization across the nine priority value chains.

## 2.3 Current State of Uganda's Manufacturing Sector

Uganda's manufacturing sector operates well below its potential. The sector currently contributes approximately **16% of GDP**, against an NDP IV target of 21% and a Vision 2040 target of 20%+. This gap reflects structural weaknesses that have persisted across successive planning cycles.

**Capacity utilization** across the manufacturing sector averages approximately **30%**. This is one of the lowest utilization rates in East Africa and reflects the combined effect of unreliable and expensive energy, weak demand, input supply constraints, skills mismatches, and limited access to finance. Low utilization means manufacturers bear high fixed costs per unit of output, making them uncompetitive against imports and unable to generate the cash flows needed for reinvestment.

Key structural characteristics of Uganda's manufacturing base include:

- **Dominance of agro-processing and light manufacturing** — the sector is concentrated in food and beverages, textiles, and building materials. Capital goods, intermediate industrial inputs, and pharmaceuticals are predominantly imported.
- **High import dependency** — Uganda imports the majority of its industrial machinery, equipment, chemicals, and processed metals. This creates chronic current account pressure and limits domestic value addition.
- **Small firm size** — most manufacturing enterprises are micro or small, with limited access to technology, skilled labour, and formal finance. Large-scale industrial production is concentrated in a small number of firms.
- **Limited export competitiveness** — manufactured exports remain a small share of total exports, dominated by a narrow range of processed agricultural commodities. Uganda has not yet established a significant presence in regional or global manufacturing value chains.

Despite these constraints, Uganda's manufacturing sector has genuine competitive foundations: a large and growing domestic market, proximity to the DRC and South Sudan as major consumer markets, a young and growing labour force, improving infrastructure connectivity, and a growing resource base in minerals and agricultural raw materials.

## 2.4 Rationale for the Nine Priority Value Chains

The nine value chains covered in this study were selected by MTIC through a deliberate prioritization process that drew on NDP IV designations, NIP sectoral priorities, Uganda's natural resource endowments, import substitution potential, and the structure of Uganda's regional export markets.

**Report 1 value chains — Iron & Steel, Copper & Allied Metals, and Automotives** — were selected based on Uganda's identified mineral resource base (iron ore, copper deposits), the large import bill Uganda incurs on steel products, fabricated metal goods, and vehicle parts, and the significant regional demand from the DRC, South Sudan, and Rwanda. These are capital-intensive chains where Uganda currently has limited domestic production but where targeted investments can catalyse significant import substitution and regional export growth.

**Report 2 value chains — Textiles & Garments, Pharmaceuticals, Petrochemicals & Fertilizers, Sugar & Confectionery, Plastics & Packaging, and Cement & Building Materials** — were selected on the basis of Uganda's existing productive base and raw material availability (cotton, sugarcane, oil and gas feedstocks, limestone), large domestic and regional demand, and strategic import substitution opportunity. These chains represent a mix of chains where Uganda already has production capacity that can be deepened and chains where new investment is needed to capture market share currently held by imports.

Across all nine chains, the analytical question is the same: given Uganda's resource base, competitive position, market access, and institutional capacity, **which 3–4 products or sub-sectors within each chain offer the most credible path to competitive manufacturing at scale?** This diagnostic does not attempt to develop all products in all chains simultaneously — that approach has historically diffused resources and produced limited results. The output is a focused prioritization.

## 2.5 NDP IV and Tenfold Targets Relevant to This Study

The following specific targets from NDP IV and the Tenfold Growth Strategy frame the ambition against which this study's recommendations are calibrated:

| Indicator | Current Baseline | NDP IV Target (2024/25) | Tenfold Target (2040) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Manufacturing share of GDP | 16% | 21% | 20%+ |
| Manufactured exports as % of total exports | ~12% | 35% | >50% |
| Capacity utilization (manufacturing) | ~30% | 60%+ | 75%+ |
| Annual manufacturing sector growth rate | ~6% | 12% | 12–15% |
| Industrial park occupancy | <40% | 70%+ | >85% |

These targets are not modest. Achieving them requires the kind of deliberate, value-chain-level analysis and investment prioritization that this study is designed to provide.
