**Status:** Draft for validation

## 10.1 Introduction

The six Report 2 value chains span agro-processing (textiles, sugar), health (pharmaceuticals), agricultural inputs and energy (fertilizers, petrochemicals), and construction-linked manufacturing (cement, plastics). Despite their diversity, they share a common set of binding constraints and a common strategic pattern: **Uganda exports or under-processes raw inputs and imports the finished product.** This chapter consolidates the cross-cutting actions that lift all six, mapped to the six PCP priority areas.

---

## 10.2 The Shared Binding Constraints

**1. Energy cost and reliability.** Cement (clinker), textiles (spinning/wet-processing), and any future petrochemical processing are energy-intensive. Uganda's industrial tariff (~5.5 US¢/kWh, 2025; ERA) is regionally competitive, but reliability and backup cost matter. Sugar co-generation is a partial in-sector solution that can ease grid pressure.

**2. Logistics and the landlocked penalty.** Logistics inefficiencies cost the economy ~USD 827 million/year and ~18–20% of the sale price of goods — affecting imported inputs (resin, APIs, clinker) and regional exports (sugar, cement, garments) alike.

**3. Access to long-term finance.** GMP pharmaceutical plants, spinning mills, clinker kilns, recycling plants, and the refinery are all highly capital-intensive. Long-term finance is scarce and costly, and **2025 liquidity tightening has crowded out private credit** (KPMG, *Budget Brief 2025/26*).

**4. Skills.** ~68% of manufacturers report difficulty finding required technical skills (UMA). Textile engineering, pharmaceutical QA/QC, chemical/process engineering, and food technology are particularly scarce.

**5. Standards and quality.** UNBS standards (food, cement, packaging) and NDA/WHO-GMP (pharmaceuticals) are simultaneously the gatekeepers to markets and the means to displace sub-standard imports. Enforcement is uneven.

**6. Raw-material / feedstock dependence.** A recurring theme: imported APIs (pharma), imported resin (plastics), imported clinker (cement), under-processed lint (textiles), and unmet fertilizer supply. Securing or substituting feedstock is the structural key across the report.

## 10.3 Cross-Cutting Priority Actions

### A. Move up the value chain from raw commodity to processed product
The defining Report 2 opportunity. Process cotton lint domestically (textiles), produce clinker (cement), make industrial sugar and ethanol from surplus cane, and substitute finished medicines and packaging. Policy should consistently favour **domestic value addition over raw-commodity export and finished-goods import.**

### B. Standards and quality infrastructure
Resource **UNBS and NDA** to enforce standards on imported textiles, medicines, cement, packaging, and fertilizer — protecting consumers and levelling the field for compliant domestic producers. The single highest-leverage cross-cutting lever for Report 2 (PCP Area 4).

### C. Guaranteed demand through public procurement
Use public procurement as a demand anchor: **locally made essential medicines** (NMS/JMS), **institutional uniforms** (garments), **domestic cement** for public infrastructure, and **fertilizer** programmes for agriculture.

### D. Industrial parks, utilities, and circularity
Develop serviced parks with reliable power, water, and **effluent treatment** (critical for textiles wet-processing and pharma), and build the **circular-economy infrastructure** (plastics recycling, EPR, sugar co-generation, waste co-processing in cement) that the NEMA strategy envisages (PCP Areas 3 & 5).

### E. Industrial-finance instrument
Scale a **long-term industrial-finance facility** (UDB + partners, blended/concessional) for the capital-intensive plants — GMP pharma, spinning, clinker, recycling — to offset the crowding-out of private credit.

### F. Skills compact
A targeted skills programme spanning **textile engineering, pharmaceutical QA/QC and regulatory affairs, chemical/process engineering, and food technology**, delivered through Makerere, UIRI, and TVET in partnership with anchor firms.

### G. Sequence the petrochemical opportunity
Treat the **Kabaale refinery / Kabalega petrochemical park** as the medium-term feedstock anchor for plastics and fertilizers, and **prepare the downstream linkages now** (recycling, blending, conversion) so that domestic resin and chemicals have ready demand when the refinery comes online (~2029–30).

## 10.5 Summary of Cross-Cutting Recommendations

1. **Favour domestic value addition** over raw-commodity export / finished-goods import, consistently across policy.
2. **Enforce UNBS and NDA standards** (highest-leverage quick win for Report 2).
3. **Anchor demand through public procurement** (medicines, uniforms, cement, fertilizer).
4. **Build parks, utilities, effluent, and circular-economy infrastructure.**
5. **Create a long-term industrial-finance facility.**
6. **Launch a cross-sector skills compact.**
7. **Sequence the petrochemical park** and prepare its downstream linkages now.

Together these convert Uganda's agricultural base, established sugar/cement industries, proven pharmaceutical capability, and emerging oil sector into a value-adding, import-substituting industrial system.
