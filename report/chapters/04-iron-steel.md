## 4.1 Value Chain Map

The iron and steel value chain runs through six sequential phases, from iron ore extraction to market distribution. Uganda currently has significant presence in the downstream phases and a critical gap in the upstream phases.

| Phase | Activity | Raw Materials | Output |
|---|---|---|---|
| I | Exploration & Mining | — | Beneficiated & graded iron ore |
| II | Ironmaking | Iron ore, coal, dolomite, limestone | Sponge iron or pig iron |
| III | Steelmaking | Sponge iron, pig iron, scrap | Crude steel (<2% carbon) |
| IV | Continuous Casting | Liquid steel | Slabs, blooms, billets |
| V | Steel Rolling | Billets, coils, wire rods, chemicals | Flat products, long products, wire products, structural steel |
| VI | Market | Finished products | Revenue |

**Value addition by phase** (per tonne of steel at USD 750):

| Phase | Incremental Value Addition |
|---|---|
| Mining & Beneficiation | 5–10% (USD 37–75) |
| Ironmaking (DRI) | 15–25% (USD 112–188) |
| Steelmaking | 20–30% (USD 150–225) |
| Continuous Casting | 10–15% (USD 75–113) |
| Steel Rolling | 10–20% (USD 75–150) |
| Fabrication & Distribution | 5–15% (USD 37–113) |

**Uganda's position in the chain:** More than 90% of Uganda's 40 active players operate in Phases V and VI — steel rolling and marketing. Two companies (Tembo Steel and Abyssinia Iron & Steel) operate in Phase II (sponge iron). Only one company (SINO Minerals) mines iron ore at commercial scale. The gap between Uganda's iron ore deposits and its downstream rolling capacity is the defining structural problem of the sector.

**Key players:**

| Company | Phase(s) | Location | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| SINO Minerals Ltd | I | Kabale (Kigezi) | Only large-scale iron ore miner; 300,000 tpa capacity |
| Tembo Steel Ltd | II, III, IV, V | Iganga / Lugazi | Largest sponge iron plant; 350,000 tpa iron ore→billets |
| Abyssinia Iron & Steel (U) Ltd | II, III, IV, V | Jinja | 30,000 tpa sponge iron; supplies parent AGI (Kenya) |
| Roofings Ltd | V | Namanve / Lubowa | Major flat & long products producer |
| Steel & Tube Industries | V | Namanve / Nakawa | Flat and wire products |
| Uganda Baati | V | Kampala | Roofing sheets |
| Pramukh Steel, Yogi Steel | III, V | Jinja / Kampala | Billets from sponge iron + scrap |

## 4.2 Current State

### Production and Capacity

| Indicator | Value |
|---|---|
| Total installed capacity | 1,682,779 tpa |
| Capacity utilized | 513,364 tpa (30.5%) |
| Liquid steel production | ~230,000 t/year (14% of installed) |
| Finished steel production | ~600,000 t/year |
| Number of active firms | 40 (up from 20 in 2020) |
| Direct employment | ~10,000 workers |
| Total sector investment | Over USD 900 million |
| Monthly power bill | UGX 15.88 billion |
| Products exported | 27% of production (98% within EAC) |

Capacity utilization at 30.5% — down from 35.3% in 2020 — reflects a sector where private investment has outpaced market development. Phase V (steel rolling) holds approximately 70% of installed capacity. The rapid growth in the number of players (from 20 to 40 in five years) is concentrated entirely in downstream rolling, creating saturation in certain product segments while upstream phases remain underdeveloped.

Liquid steel production of 230,000 t/year against an installed downstream capacity requiring over 600,000 t/year of liquid steel creates a structural deficit of approximately 370,000 t/year, which is met by imported billets and coils. This deficit is the direct consequence of absent upstream investment in Phases I and II.

### Iron Ore Resource Base

Uganda holds estimated iron ore reserves of **350 million tonnes**. High-grade hematite ore (60–65% Fe, among the highest grades globally) is located in south-western Uganda (Kigezi region — Kabale, Rubanda, Kisoro). Lower-grade magnetite ore occurs in the eastern region. Of 44 licenses issued for iron ore-related activities since 2019, only 2 companies are actively mining: SINO Minerals (large scale, 300,000 tpa) and Great Lakes Iron & Steel Company (small scale). The resource base is substantial; its commercial exploitation is minimal.

### Trade Position

| Trade indicator | Value (2023) |
|---|---|
| Iron & steel imports | USD 527.14 million |
| — of which flat rolled products | USD 285.7 million (54.2% of imports) |
| — of which bars & rods | USD 70.63 million (13.4%) |
| — of which ferrous waste & scrap | USD 49.65 million (9.4%) |

Import sources: Japan (24%), China (19%), Kenya (18.7%). The largest import category — flat rolled products at USD 285.7 million — is also a product Uganda has the capacity to produce domestically if upstream phases are developed.

Uganda's per capita steel consumption stands at **15.3 kg** (2020), against the African average of 27 kg and the global average of 219.3 kg. This low baseline reflects both the early stage of Uganda's industrialization and the scale of the domestic demand opportunity as incomes, construction, and infrastructure investment expand.

## 4.3 Binding Constraints

### Constraint 1: Underdeveloped Upstream — The Core Structural Problem

The fundamental constraint is the absence of commercial-scale iron ore mining and sponge iron production at a level sufficient to supply downstream rolling mills. Uganda has world-class iron ore but only one commercial miner (SINO, 300,000 tpa capacity), two sponge iron plants (Tembo: 350,000 tpa; Abyssinia: 30,000 tpa), and a downstream sector that requires at least 750,000 tpa of iron ore to meet current domestic steel demand without imports. Meeting short- and medium-term demand requires a commercial iron ore mine of at least **1,000,000 tpa** capacity, estimated at **USD 6.73 million** to establish.

### Constraint 2: Power Unreliability

Unreliable electricity is the most frequently cited operational constraint across all 40 firms surveyed. Estimated annual losses to the sector:

| Loss category | Annual value |
|---|---|
| Lost productivity | UGX 20 billion |
| Spoilt material | UGX 12 billion |
| Equipment damage | UGX 7 billion |
| **Total** | **UGX 39 billion (~USD 10 million)** |

Uganda's industrial electricity tariff of **8–10 US cents/kWh** is higher than Kenya (5–7 cents), Tanzania (6–8 cents), and Egypt (4–5 cents). A pilot 5-cent tariff at two industrial parks (2022–2023) was discontinued because production did not increase in response — confirming that tariff reduction alone does not unlock productivity. The outdated transmission network causing voltage drops and unplanned outages is the primary constraint; grid reliability must be addressed alongside any tariff reform.

### Constraint 3: Market Saturation and Missed Procurement Linkage

The downstream rolling phase is oversaturated, particularly in wire products. New capacity has outpaced market growth, compressing margins and driving quality-cutting practices. More significantly, national infrastructure projects — dams, expressways, the Standard Gauge Railway — have historically imported the steel they require. Identified foregone domestic steel procurement across key completed and planned projects: **USD 130 million**. Without directed procurement policy, the domestic market cannot absorb existing installed capacity.

### Constraint 4: Standards Enforcement Gap

Poor enforcement enables substandard imported and local steel to undercut compliant manufacturers. UNBS's first engineering laboratory is under construction but not yet equipped (funding not approved). Without a functional national testing laboratory, Uganda cannot enforce PVoC requirements across all steel categories or build the quality assurance system needed for export competitiveness.

### Constraint 5: High Cost of Capital

Uganda's average commercial interest rate of **18–23%** is the highest in the region (Kenya: 13–17%; South Africa: 9–11%). Capital-intensive upstream investment in mining and sponge iron making is acutely sensitive to the cost of long-term debt. Uganda Development Bank has significantly reduced lending to the sector, pushing firms onto commercial terms.

### Constraint 6: Logistics — Transport and Ore Movement

Transport cost of **USD 0.08–0.12 per ton/km** (Kenya: 0.06–0.09; South Africa: 0.04–0.06). Iron ore travels over 550 km from Kigezi mines to Kampala/Jinja facilities entirely by road. No rail connection links mining areas to industrial zones. The absence of railway is a cost multiplier that makes Ugandan iron ore more expensive to process than imported billets in the short run — the inverse of what the resource endowment should produce.

### Constraint 7: Policy and Regulatory Inconsistency

Inconsistent tax policy (sudden import restrictions, HS code disputes, individual CPC letters rather than sector-wide tariff rules), the un-operationalized Competition Act, and complex overlapping incentive regimes create an unstable operating environment. The Competition Act's non-operationalization allows large vertically integrated players to operate across the entire value chain in ways that squeeze smaller Phase V mills out of raw material supply.

## 4.4 Market Assessment

### Domestic Market

Uganda's domestic steel demand is driven by construction, infrastructure, and manufacturing. Current apparent consumption is approximately 600,000 tonnes per year. Directed government procurement represents the most immediately actionable market lever:

| Project | Steel required | USD value |
|---|---|---|
| Standard Gauge Railway (338 km track) | 92,000 tonnes | USD 72 million |
| Kiira Motors buses — frames only (to 2028) | 37,300 tonnes | USD 75 million |
| Major completed projects (dams, expressways) | 88,000 tonnes | USD 62 million |
| **Total identified** | **~217,300 tonnes** | **~USD 209 million** |

Directing this procurement to domestic suppliers does not require new investment — it requires a policy decision.

### Regional Market (EAC)

The EAC iron and steel import market is **USD 3.5 billion** per year, growing from USD 1.44 billion over the past decade. Uganda captures only USD 153 million — 0.04% of this market. A 10% market share target represents **USD 350 million** in annual export revenue. Uganda's central EAC location, and improving road corridor access to DRC, South Sudan, Rwanda, Tanzania, and Kenya, provide structural positioning advantages for regional supply.

| EAC country | Annual iron & steel imports |
|---|---|
| Kenya | USD 1,284 million |
| Tanzania | USD 706 million |
| Uganda | USD 604 million |
| DRC | USD 214 million |
| Rwanda | USD 162 million |
| Burundi | USD 89 million |
| South Sudan | USD 38 million |

### Export Competitiveness

Uganda's sole current export cost advantage is **labour** (USD 150–250/month vs Kenya USD 250–400, South Africa USD 500–800). On electricity, transport, and capital costs, Uganda is less competitive than all regional peers. The pathway to export competitiveness runs through upstream development: **local iron ore processing reduces production cost by USD 30–50 per tonne of steel** relative to imported raw material routes.

| Production route | Cost per tonne of billet (USD) |
|---|---|
| DRI from local ore (coal) | 520 |
| 100% local scrap | 565 |
| 100% imported scrap | 676 |
| Imported billets (market) | ~700–750 |

## 4.5 Prioritized Products

Applying the diagnostic principle — Uganda must identify 3 to 4 products to concentrate its industrial effort, not attempt to develop the full value chain simultaneously — the following priorities are identified.

### Priority 1: Commercial Iron Ore Mining (Phase I)

**What:** Establish commercial iron ore mining at a minimum of 1,000,000 tpa, anchored in Kigezi (Kabale/Rubanda/Kisoro).

**Why:** Everything downstream depends on this. SINO Minerals at 300,000 tpa cannot supply the two existing sponge iron plants at capacity, let alone the six additional firms planning Phase II investment. Uganda cannot reduce its USD 374 million steel trade deficit while mining its own ore at less than 30% of the required volume.

**How:** UNMC and UDC lead, either through direct investment in a new mine (USD 6.73 million) or equity partnership to scale SINO Minerals' existing capacity. Target: 1,000,000 tpa operational within 24 months.

**Market:** Captive domestic demand from sponge iron producers. Potential regional supply as EAC sponge iron capacity expands.

### Priority 2: Sponge Iron and Billets (Phases II–IV)

**What:** Scale domestic sponge iron production and billet casting to eliminate Uganda's dependency on imported billets.

**Why:** Imported billets cost USD 700–750/tonne. Billets from local ore cost USD 520/tonne — a saving of USD 180–230/tonne. Six established firms have stated plans to invest in Phase II once ore supply is secured. UDC equity partnerships with Tembo Steel (scale from 350,000 to 700,000 tpa) and Abyssinia would accelerate this phase.

**How:** UDC equity co-investment with Tembo Steel and Abyssinia. Coal import tax streamlining to reduce input costs. Captive supply agreement structure to de-risk private investment.

**Market:** Domestic rolling mills (current import bill: significant share of USD 527 million). EAC regional billet supply as neighbouring countries build rolling capacity without upstream production.

### Priority 3: Bars, Rods, and Wire Products (Phase V)

**What:** Deepen Uganda's established production of bars, rods (TMX/TMT rebars, round bars), and wire products (barbed wire, binding wire, BRC mesh, chain link, nails).

**Why:** Uganda already produces and exports these products. Imports total USD 70.63 million per year — the second largest steel import category. EAC demand is growing with regional construction expansion. With secure domestic billet supply from Priority 2, bars and rods become directly cost-competitive with imports from Japan, China, and Kenya.

**How:** UNBS standards enforcement to clear substandard imports; directed national project procurement for rebars; support Phase V(b) smaller mills to expand upcountry and regional market reach.

**Market:** USD 70.63 million domestic import substitution; EAC export growth (Uganda already exports to DRC, South Sudan, Rwanda, Tanzania, Kenya).

### Priority 4: Light Gauge Flat Products — Roofing Sheets and Coated Sheets (Phase V, targeted)

**What:** Expand production of roofing sheets, corrugated iron, galvanized sheets, and light gauge flat products — the product categories already manufactured by Roofings Ltd, Uganda Baati, and East African Roofing Systems.

**Why:** Flat rolled products are Uganda's largest steel import category at USD 285.7 million per year. Uganda has significant existing production capacity. Reducing raw material costs through Priority 2 makes domestic flat product production more competitive. Full Hot Rolled Coil (HRC) production from slabs is explicitly excluded at this stage: it requires 200+ MW of stable power and USD 500M+ in CAPEX — beyond Uganda's current grid and financing capacity.

**How:** Utilize existing rolling capacity more intensively; improve domestic coil and slit coil supply from Tembo Steel; enforce standards on imported substandard flat products.

**Market:** USD 285.7 million domestic import substitution; EAC roofing and construction materials markets.

### What is Deprioritized and Why

| Product / Sub-sector | Reason for deprioritization |
|---|---|
| Full Hot Rolled Coil (HRC) production from slabs | Requires 200+ MW stable power and USD 500M+ CAPEX; grid and financing conditions not yet met |
| Specialty steels (stainless, high-speed, tool steel) | Insufficient domestic market; requires advanced metallurgy capability not yet present |
| Export beyond EAC markets (near term) | Production costs not yet competitive with China and India on global markets |
| Artisanal iron ore mining scale-up | Bulk ore supply requires engineered mine design; artisanal mining cannot provide consistent grade and volume |
