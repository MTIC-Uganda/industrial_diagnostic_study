## 9.A Value Chain Map

The cement chain runs from limestone quarrying through clinker production to cement grinding, with adjacent building-materials products (concrete, blocks, fibre-cement, tiles, aggregates). Uganda has a **strong cement-grinding industry** but a **critical clinker gap** — it imports more than half its clinker, the highest-value step.

| Phase | Activity | Main inputs | Output |
|---|---|---|---|
| I | Limestone quarrying | Limestone deposits | Limestone |
| II | Clinker production | Limestone, energy, additives | Clinker |
| III | Cement grinding | Clinker, gypsum, additives | Cement (OPC, blended) |
| IV | Downstream products | Cement, aggregates | Concrete, blocks, tiles, fibre-cement |
| V | Market | Products | Construction |

**Where value is captured and lost.** Uganda has substantial grinding capacity but **imports over 50% of its clinker** — the most capital- and energy-intensive, highest-value step. Cement and clinker (HS 2523) imports were about **USD 162.2 million in 2024**, against exports of about **USD 86.0 million** (ITC TradeMap) — Uganda is both a significant exporter of finished cement to neighbours *and* a major importer of clinker, the classic "missing middle." Closing the clinker gap is the value-addition prize.

## 9.B Current State Assessment

**Market.** Uganda's cement market reached about **USD 707 million in 2024, up ~12% year-on-year** (IndexBox), driven by construction, infrastructure, and the oil-and-gas build-out.

**Capacity and firms.** Installed cement (grinding) capacity has grown well beyond the ~6.8 million tpa of 2018. Major producers (Wikipedia plant list; CemNet):
- **Tororo Cement** — the largest, ~3.0 million tpa.
- **Hima Cement** — ~2.0 million tpa across Hima (Kasese), Namanve blending, and Tororo grinding; owns the **Dura limestone quarry (Kamwenge)**, with reserves estimated sufficient to ~2036.
- **Simba Cement (Uganda)** — ~1.0 million tpa; backed by a new clinker plant in West Pokot, Kenya (2024).
- **Kampala Cement** — ~1.0 million tpa.
- New capacity: a **USD 300 million plant in north-eastern Uganda** (>6,000 t clinker/day) and **West International Holding's USD 200 million Buikwe plant** (~4,000 t/day, due 2025) — both adding clinker capacity to close the import gap (Ecofin Agency; CemNet, 2024–25).

**Clinker dependence.** Despite strong grinding capacity, Uganda imports **>50% of its clinker** — the structural weakness the new plants aim to address.

**Trade position (ITC TradeMap, 2024).** Cement & clinker (HS 2523): imports ~USD 162.2m, exports ~USD 86.0m. Uganda exports finished cement to **Rwanda, Democratic Republic of Congo (DRC), South Sudan, and Kenya** while importing clinker — a chain that is competitive downstream but leaks value upstream.

## 9.C Binding Constraints Analysis

| Dimension | Assessment |
|---|---|
| **Inputs / raw materials** | Limestone reserves exist (Dura/Kamwenge, Tororo, Karamoja) but clinker capacity has lagged grinding — hence >50% clinker imports. New plants address this. |
| **Technology & productivity** | Grinding is well-established; clinker kilns are capital- and energy-intensive; the new plants bring modern capacity. |
| **Energy** | Clinker production is highly energy-intensive (thermal + electricity); energy cost and reliability are decisive for competitiveness. |
| **Water** | Process water managed; not the binding constraint. |
| **Logistics** | Cement is bulk/low-value-density; the landlocked penalty (~18–20% of sale price) both protects the domestic market and limits distant exports — favouring domestic clinker over imports. |
| **Waste & effluent** | Kiln dust and emissions managed under NEMA; co-processing of waste (alternative fuels) is an opportunity. |
| **Finance & investment** | Clinker plants are very capital-intensive; recent USD 200–300m investments show appetite. |
| **Skills & workforce** | Process and maintenance skills needed; sector has a trained base. |
| **Standards & quality** | UNBS cement standards; sub-standard cement is a safety and market issue. |
| **Policy & regulatory** | Limestone licensing, energy policy, and EAC trade rules shape competitiveness; clinker-import reliance is the central strategic concern. |

## 9.D Market Assessment

**Domestic.** Large and growing (USD 707m, +12% in 2024), driven by construction, infrastructure, and oil-and-gas. Domestic demand comfortably supports closing the clinker gap.

**Regional (EAC/COMESA).** Uganda is already a competitive **finished-cement exporter** to Rwanda, DRC, South Sudan, and Kenya (USD 86m in 2024) — proximity favours Uganda in these markets. Reliable domestic clinker would deepen this advantage.

**Global.** Not relevant — cement is a regional product by economics.

**Accessible market conclusion.** Prioritize **domestic clinker production** (close the >50% import gap), **defend and grow regional finished-cement exports**, and develop **higher-value building materials** (fibre-cement, tiles, precast).

## 9.E Prioritized Products

| Product / sub-sector | Market | Comp. adv. | Feasibility | Jobs | Import subst. | Priority |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Clinker production (close the import gap)** | High (USD 162m imports) | High (limestone) | Medium | High | Very high | **Priority** |
| **Finished cement (defend & grow regional export)** | High (USD 86m export) | High | High | High | Achieved | **Priority** |
| **Higher-value building materials (fibre-cement, tiles, precast)** | Medium–High | Medium | Medium | High | High | **Priority** |
| Specialty/white cement | Low | Low | Low | Low | Low | Deprioritize |

**The three priorities for Cement & Building Materials:**

1. **Clinker production** — the single highest-value step and the chain's structural gap; the new USD 200–300m plants should be supported to substitute the USD 162m clinker/cement import line and secure feedstock for domestic grinders.
2. **Finished cement for regional export** — defend and grow Uganda's competitive USD 86m export position to neighbours, strengthened by reliable domestic clinker.
3. **Higher-value building materials** — fibre-cement sheets, tiles, and precast/concrete products that add value and substitute imports in construction.

**Explicit deprioritization.** Uganda should **not** target niche specialty or white-cement production — small markets requiring specialised, costly capacity. The strategic priority is **mainstream clinker + cement + building materials** for domestic and regional markets.
