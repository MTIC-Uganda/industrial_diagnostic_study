## 9.0 Why Cement & Building Materials Matters for the Tenfold Growth Strategy

You cannot build a ten-times-larger economy without cement — it is the literal foundation of the infrastructure, factories, housing and cities that tenfold growth requires. Uganda already has a **strong, regionally-competitive cement-grinding and finished-cement export industry**, but it **imports over half its clinker** — the highest-value, most capital- and energy-intensive step. Closing that clinker gap captures the value Uganda currently sends abroad, secures the supply that a construction boom needs, and — done with modern, lower-carbon kilns — turns a heavy-emitting industry into a competitiveness asset.

## 9.A Value Chain Map

| Phase | Activity | Main inputs | Output | Uganda's position |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| I | Limestone quarrying | Limestone deposits | Limestone | **Adequate** (Dura, Tororo, Karamoja) |
| II | Clinker production | Limestone, energy, additives | Clinker | **Gap** — >50% imported |
| III | Cement grinding | Clinker, gypsum, additives | Cement (OPC, blended) | **Strong** |
| IV | Downstream products | Cement, aggregates | Concrete, blocks, tiles, fibre-cement | **Growing** |
| V | Market | Products | Construction | **Active**; regional exporter |

**Where value is captured and lost.** Uganda has substantial grinding capacity but **imports over 50% of its clinker** — the highest-value step. Cement and clinker (HS 2523) imports were ~**USD 162.2 million in 2024**, against exports of ~**USD 86.0 million** (ITC TradeMap): Uganda is both a significant **finished-cement exporter** to neighbours *and* a major **clinker importer** — the classic "missing middle." Closing the clinker gap is the prize.

## 9.C Market Analysis (four levels) and 2040 Projection

| Level | Size / Uganda position | Drivers |
|---|---|---|
| **Uganda (domestic)** | ~USD 707m (2024), +12%/yr; comfortably supports closing the clinker gap | Construction, infrastructure, oil & gas, housing |
| **Regional (EAC + COMESA)** | Competitive finished-cement exporter (USD 86m, 2024) to Rwanda, DRC, S. Sudan, Kenya; proximity favours Uganda | Regional construction; corridor proximity |
| **Continental (Africa)** | Cement is regional by economics; AfCFTA matters mainly for neighbouring markets | Regional infrastructure |
| **Global** | Not relevant — cement is a regional product | — |

### 2040 projection — Tenfold logic

A ten-times-larger economy is a construction supercycle. At ~12%/yr the market roughly doubles within ~6 years; sustained infrastructure, urbanisation and housing to 2040 imply a **domestic cement market of several billion USD a year** — far beyond today's USD 707m. The strategic imperative is to meet that with **domestic clinker** (not imports) and to **grow regional finished-cement exports**, while moving into **higher-value building materials**. Other markets grow on business-as-usual; Uganda's external play is **regional finished cement**.

## 9.D Binding Constraints — Weighted

| # | Constraint (category) | Weight | Evidence / gravity |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Clinker capacity gap (inputs/technology)** | **5** | >50% of clinker imported despite strong grinding — the chain's structural weakness; USD 162m import line |
| 2 | **Energy cost & reliability (energy)** | **5** | Clinker is highly energy-intensive (thermal + power); energy cost/reliability is decisive for competitiveness |
| 3 | **Capital intensity (finance)** | **4** | Clinker kilns are very capital-intensive; recent USD 200–300m investments show appetite but capital is the gate |
| 4 | **Limestone licensing & land (inputs/policy)** | **3** | Reserves exist (Dura, Tororo, Karamoja) but licensing/land access must keep pace with clinker expansion |
| 5 | **Logistics (infrastructure)** | **3** | Bulk/low-value-density product; landlocked penalty (~18–20%) protects the domestic market but limits distant export — favouring domestic clinker |
| 6 | **Kiln emissions / dust (waste)** | **3** | Cement is carbon- and dust-intensive; NEMA-regulated; also an alternative-fuel opportunity |
| 7 | **Standards (standards)** | **2** | UNBS cement standards; sub-standard cement a safety/market issue |

The two critical constraints — **clinker capacity** and **energy** — are linked: new clinker kilns only pay off if energy is affordable and reliable, so power agreements and alternative fuels must accompany the capacity build-out.

## 9.E Greening & Sustainability

Cement is among the most carbon-intensive industries globally (~8% of world CO₂), so greening is both a **cost lever** (energy is the biggest cost) and a **market-access** play (green building, carbon border measures).

| Dimension | Status / opportunity |
|---|---|
| **Current status** | Energy-intensive kilns; clinker import reliance; kiln dust/emissions managed under NEMA |
| **Current solutions** | Blended cements (some clinker substitution); NEMA emissions regulation |
| **Emerging solutions (competitiveness enablers)** | (1) **Alternative fuels / co-processing** of waste and biomass in kilns (cuts energy cost *and* emissions, links to the Plastics/waste chain); (2) **clinker substitution with supplementary cementitious materials** — **steel slag (Chapter 4)**, **phosphogypsum (Chapter 6)**, pozzolana — lowering both cost and carbon; (3) **energy efficiency & waste-heat recovery**; (4) **green/blended cement** product lines |

**Why it pays:** every tonne of clinker substituted by slag/phosphogypsum/pozzolana cuts the most expensive, most carbon-heavy input — and creates a circular link that consumes by-products from the steel and fertilizer chains. Green cement is cheaper cement here.

## 9.F Prioritized Products — through the Tenfold Lens

| Product / sub-sector | Market | Comp. adv. | Feasibility | Jobs | Import subst. | Green fit | Priority |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Clinker production (close the gap)** | High (USD 162m imports) | High (limestone) | Medium | High | Very high | Medium (alt-fuel/SCM) | **Priority** |
| **Finished cement (defend & grow export)** | High (USD 86m export) | High | High | High | Achieved | Medium–High (blended) | **Priority** |
| **Higher-value building materials (fibre-cement, tiles, precast)** | Medium–High | Medium | Medium | High | High | Medium | **Priority** |
| Specialty / white cement | Low | Low | Low | Low | Low | — | Deprioritize |

**The three priorities for Cement & Building Materials:**

1. **Clinker production** — the highest-value step and the chain's structural gap; support the new USD 200–300m plants to substitute the USD 162m import line and secure feedstock for domestic grinders, with alternative fuels and SCM substitution built in.
2. **Finished cement for regional export** — defend and grow Uganda's competitive USD 86m export position, strengthened by reliable domestic clinker.
3. **Higher-value building materials** — fibre-cement, tiles and precast/concrete products that add value and substitute construction imports.

**Explicit deprioritization.** Uganda should **not** target niche specialty/white cement (small markets, costly specialised capacity). The priority is **mainstream clinker + cement + building materials** for domestic and regional markets.

## 9.G Project Profiles

**P1 — Domestic clinker capacity build-out (strategic anchor + green).**
- *Strategic:* closes the >50% clinker gap; secures feedstock for grinders; substitutes USD 162m imports.
- *Quick-win element:* support commissioning of the NE and Buikwe plants already underway.
- *Green:* alternative-fuel co-processing; SCM (slag/phosphogypsum) clinker substitution.

**P2 — Green/blended cement & SCM utilisation (green + strategic).**
- *Strategic:* lowers cost and carbon; circular link to steel slag (Ch.4) and phosphogypsum (Ch.6).
- *Quick-win element:* blended-cement product lines using existing by-products.
- *Green:* the flagship low-carbon-cement move.

**P3 — Higher-value building materials (strategic).**
- *Strategic:* fibre-cement, tiles, precast — substitutes construction imports; job-rich.
- *Quick-win element:* build on existing cement base.
- *Green:* uses blended/low-carbon cement.
