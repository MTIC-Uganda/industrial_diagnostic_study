## 7.A Value Chain Map

The sugar chain runs from sugarcane cultivation through milling to refined sugar, with downstream branches into industrial sugar, confectionery, ethanol, and co-generation. Uganda has a **mature, competitive milling industry** and, unusually for Report 2, is in **structural surplus** — making the strategic question one of **downstream diversification**, not raw expansion.

| Phase | Activity | Main inputs | Output |
|---|---|---|---|
| I | Cane growing | Land, inputs | Sugarcane |
| II | Milling | Cane | Raw/brown sugar, molasses, bagasse |
| III | Refining | Raw sugar | Refined/industrial sugar |
| IV | Downstream | Sugar, molasses, bagasse | Confectionery, ethanol, electricity (co-gen) |
| V | Market | Products | Domestic + regional sales |

**Where value is captured and lost.** Uganda produces a surplus of brown sugar but still imports some **industrial/refined sugar** and confectionery, and under-exploits downstream products (ethanol, co-generation, confectionery). Sugar imports (HS 1701) were about **USD 38.1 million in 2024** — largely industrial sugar not yet made domestically at sufficient scale — even as the country exports surplus brown sugar regionally (ITC TradeMap).

## 7.B Current State Assessment

**Production and surplus.** National sugar output reached about **822,000 tonnes (2022)** — roughly 720,000 t brown table sugar and 102,000 t industrial sugar — against domestic consumption of about **370,000 tonnes**, leaving a structural **surplus of 180,000+ tonnes** (sector data; Wikipedia *Sugar industry in Uganda*). The surplus, plus continued licensing of new mills, has created over-supply and price pressure — the live policy issue in the sector.

**Firms.** **Kakira Sugar Works** is the largest producer (around half of national output, crushing over 2 million tonnes of cane/year), followed by **Kinyara Sugar** (industrial-sugar refinery expanded to ~75,000 t in 2023), **Sugar Corporation of Uganda (SCOUL)**, **Mayuge Sugar**, and a growing number of smaller mills (USMA; Kakira; Food Business MEA, 2023).

**Downstream.** Molasses supports ethanol production; bagasse supports co-generation (electricity to the grid); confectionery is a growing but still substantially import-supplied segment.

**Trade position (ITC TradeMap, 2024).** Sugar (HS 1701) imports ~USD 38.1m (mostly industrial sugar); regional exports of brown sugar to EAC neighbours. Uganda is a **net surplus producer of brown sugar but a net importer of industrial/specialty sugar and confectionery**.

## 7.C Binding Constraints Analysis

| Dimension | Assessment |
|---|---|
| **Inputs / raw materials** | Cane supply is ample (surplus); out-grower coordination and cane quality vary; over-licensing strains cane supply per mill. |
| **Technology & productivity** | Milling is mature; downstream (refining to industrial grade, ethanol, confectionery) needs technology investment. |
| **Energy** | Co-generation is an opportunity (bagasse to grid), turning a constraint into a revenue line. |
| **Water** | Process water managed; effluent from distilleries (ethanol) needs treatment (NEMA). |
| **Logistics** | Regional export of bulk sugar bears the landlocked penalty; domestic distribution is adequate. |
| **Waste & effluent** | Distillery effluent (vinasse) and bagasse ash require management; circular-economy potential. |
| **Finance & investment** | Downstream plants (refining, ethanol, confectionery) need capital; milling is well-capitalised. |
| **Skills & workforce** | Food-technology and process skills needed for downstream diversification. |
| **Standards & quality** | Food-safety and industrial-sugar specifications (UNBS) gate the industrial and export markets. |
| **Policy & regulatory** | The Sugar Act, licensing/zoning of new mills, and the over-supply problem are the central policy questions; export coordination matters. |

## 7.D Market Assessment

**Domestic.** Brown sugar is over-supplied; the genuine domestic gaps are **industrial/refined sugar** (for beverages, pharmaceuticals, food processing — the USD 38m import line) and **confectionery**.

**Regional (EAC/COMESA).** Surplus brown sugar finds regional markets (Kenya, South Sudan, DRC), subject to EAC trade dynamics and periodic restrictions. Industrial sugar and ethanol have regional demand.

**Global.** Uganda is not a globally competitive raw-sugar exporter; the global market is distorted and scale-driven. The realistic horizon is **regional brown-sugar export plus domestic downstream diversification**.

**Accessible market conclusion.** Prioritize **downstream diversification** — industrial sugar, ethanol, co-generation, confectionery — to absorb the surplus into higher-value products, rather than expanding raw brown-sugar capacity.

## 7.E Prioritized Products

| Product / sub-sector | Market | Comp. adv. | Feasibility | Jobs | Import subst. | Priority |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Industrial / refined sugar** | High (USD 38m imports) | High (surplus cane) | High | Medium | High | **Priority** |
| **Ethanol (fuel & potable/industrial)** | High (blending, regional) | High (molasses) | High | Medium | High | **Priority** |
| **Co-generation (bagasse to grid)** | Medium–High | High (bagasse) | High | Low | Energy benefit | **Priority** |
| **Confectionery** | Medium | Medium | Medium | High | Medium | Conditional |
| Raw brown-sugar capacity expansion | Low (surplus) | — | — | — | — | Deprioritize |

**The three priorities for Sugar & Confectionery:**

1. **Industrial/refined sugar** — substitute the USD 38m industrial-sugar import line using surplus domestic cane; serves beverages, food processing, and pharmaceuticals.
2. **Ethanol** — convert molasses surplus into fuel-blending ethanol (energy security) and industrial/potable alcohol, with strong regional demand.
3. **Co-generation** — monetise bagasse as grid electricity, improving mill economics and adding renewable power.

**Explicit deprioritization.** Uganda should **not** license or expand additional raw **brown-sugar** capacity — the sector is already in surplus and over-supply is depressing prices. Confectionery is a conditional priority (private-led, market-driven). The strategic move is **downstream, not more raw sugar.**
