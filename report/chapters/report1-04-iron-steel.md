## 4.0 Why Iron & Steel Matters for the Tenfold Growth Strategy

Steel is the physical backbone of a ten-times-larger economy. Every pillar of the Tenfold Growth Strategy — industrialisation, infrastructure, urbanisation and agro-processing — consumes steel: rebar and sections for buildings, dams and railways; plate and coil for machinery and vehicles; wire and roofing for housing and agriculture. **Uganda cannot grow its economy tenfold while importing the steel that growth is built from.** The country already holds the foundations — an estimated **350 million tonnes of iron-ore reserves** (high-grade hematite in the south-west; magnetite in the east), over **USD 1 billion** of installed industry investment, and a proven downstream manufacturing base — yet it still produces almost no steel from its own ore (NPA/UDC, 2025). Closing that gap is therefore not a sector ambition but a national precondition.

This chapter runs in diagnostic order: **what exists today (4.B) → what the market will demand to 2040 (4.C) → what constrains the chain (4.D) → how it grows green (4.E) → what to prioritise and build (4.F–4.H).**

## 4.A Value Chain Map

The iron and steel value chain runs through six sequential phases. Uganda is strong **downstream** (Phase V rolling and finishing) and critically weak **upstream** (Phases I–III: mining, ironmaking, primary steelmaking) (NPA/UDC, 2025).

| Phase | Activity | Main inputs | Output | Uganda's position |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| I | Exploration & mining | — | Beneficiated, graded iron ore | **Gap** — minimal commercial mining; small-scale only |
| II | Ironmaking | Iron ore, coal/charcoal, dolomite, limestone | Sponge iron (DRI) / pig iron | **Emerging** — ~5% of players; ~400,000 tpa |
| III | Steelmaking | Sponge iron, pig iron, scrap | Crude steel | **Weak** — largely scrap-based induction |
| IV | Continuous casting | Liquid steel | Billets, blooms, slabs | **Partial** — some billet casting |
| V | Rolling & finishing | Billets, slabs, coil, wire rod | Long, flat, tubular, wire, structural & roofing | **Strong (long); absent (flat)** — ~70% of capacity |
| VI | Market | Finished products | Domestic & regional sales | **Active** — domestic + S. Sudan/DRC export |

**Where value is captured and lost.** Uganda's mills concentrate in Phase V and run mostly on **imported semi-finished steel** (billets/slabs, HS 7207) and **scrap** (HS 7204), because Phase I–III capacity is minimal. The trade data is unambiguous: Uganda **exported USD 35.8 million of raw iron ore (HS 2601) in 2024, almost entirely to Kenya, while importing essentially none** — ore leaves unprocessed and steel returns as imports — and **imported USD 78.5 million of semi-finished steel (HS 7207)**, up sharply from USD 43.0 million in 2023 (ITC TradeMap, 2024).

**The cost of the upstream gap is quantifiable.** NPA/UDC (2025) cost modelling shows the production route Uganda mostly uses — billet from **imported scrap — is the most expensive of all at ~USD 676/tonne**, while billet from a **domestic gas-based DRI route costs ~USD 478/tonne** — roughly a **30% cost penalty** for not making iron domestically:

| Production route (per tonne) | Material | Energy | Other | **Total (USD/t)** |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| DRI (gas) | 104 | 147 | 11 | **262** |
| DRI (coal) | 130 | 160 | 9 | **299** |
| Liquid steel — 100% gas-DRI charge | 318 | 100 | 38 | **456** |
| Liquid steel — 100% local scrap | 423 | 85 | 35 | **544** |
| Liquid steel — 100% imported scrap | 534 | 85 | 35 | **655** |
| **Billet — gas-DRI route** | — | — | — | **478** |
| **Billet — imported-scrap route** | — | — | — | **676** |

*Source: NPA/UDC 2025 (cost modelling).* The single largest value-addition steps — **ironmaking and steelmaking** — are precisely the steps Uganda has barely begun, and precisely where the cost and the tenfold opportunity concentrate.

### Trade position (ITC TradeMap, 2024)

| Segment (HS) | Imports 2024 | Exports 2024 | Reading |
|---|---|---|---|

Uganda is **competitive in finished long products (rebar)** — ~USD 56m exported in 2024 to South Sudan and the DRC — but a **major net importer of the upstream and flat-steel segments** it cannot yet produce, and it **exports its iron ore raw**.

### Investment momentum (2025)

- **Tembo Steel** commissioned a second DRI (sponge iron) plant in January 2025 (~250,000 t), deepening Phase II (State House Uganda, 2025).
- **Devki Group** broke ground in November 2025 on a **USD 500 million** vertically integrated steel complex (Tororo/Mbarara), projected to create ~15,000 jobs (GMK Center, 2025; The Independent, 2025).
- Government is also pursuing a **partnership with a Kenyan steel manufacturer** to help bridge Phase II (NPA/UDC, 2025).

These target exactly the missing upstream steps — the foundation any tenfold-scale plan should build on.

### Current demand is real and documented

Public infrastructure alone is a large, concrete steel market (NPA/UDC, 2025):

| Project | Scale | Steel (tonnes) | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| Karuma Dam | 600 MW | 36,000 | Completed |
| Bujagali Dam | 250 MW | 15,000 | Completed |
| Isimba Dam | 183 MW | 11,000 | Completed |
| Entebbe Expressway | 51.36 km | 21,472 | Completed |
| Jinja Nile Bridge | 0.525 km | 4,487 | Completed |
| **Standard Gauge Railway** (Kampala–Malaba) | 338 km | **92,000** (86,000 rebar + 6,000 structural) | Planned |

And domestic vehicle manufacturing is an emerging steel buyer: Kiira Motors' bus programme alone is projected to need **~37,300 tonnes of steel for bus frames by 2027/28**, implying **~USD 75 million of steel imports for bus frames alone by 2028** if not produced domestically (NPA/UDC, 2025).

### Market by level

| Level | Size / Uganda position | Drivers |
|---|---|---|
| **Uganda (domestic)** | ~0.9–1.1 Mtpa apparent consumption (confirm: UBOS); long products met domestically, flat & semi-finished imported | Construction, infrastructure (SGR, dams, roads, housing), urbanisation |
| **Regional (EAC + COMESA)** | Intra-EAC trade ~USD 12bn (2023), rising toward ~USD 18bn by mid-2025; Uganda competitive in rebar to S. Sudan & eastern DRC (USD 55.8m, 2024) | Reconstruction (S. Sudan, DRC), CET protection |
| **Continental (Africa)** | African steel demand ~35–40 Mtpa with a large import-met deficit; AfCFTA opens access | Africa-wide infrastructure deficit, AfCFTA |
| **Global** | ~1,892 Mt/yr (World Steel 2025), China-dominated; commodity scale Uganda cannot match | Not accessible for Ugandan commodity-steel export |

### 2040 projection — Uganda on the Tenfold logic

Not "today × 10." Steel intensity rises with income then plateaus. Uganda uses ~**20–25 kg of steel per capita** today, far below the world average (~230 kg) and below lower-middle-income peers (~100–150 kg). A tenfold economy implies structural transformation that pulls per-capita use toward peer levels:

| Driver | Today | 2040 (tenfold logic) |
|---|---|---|
| Per-capita steel use | ~20–25 kg | ~90–130 kg |
| Population | ~46–48m | ~60m+ |
| **Implied annual steel demand** | **~1 Mtpa** | **~5.5–8 Mtpa** |

**Strategic implication:** Uganda must plan now to *create and secure* a 5–8 Mtpa steel market — first by substituting today's flat and semi-finished imports, then by anchoring integrated, ore-based capacity ahead of demand. Other markets are projected on business-as-usual growth (~3–5%/yr regionally), where Uganda's realistic role is finished long products to neighbouring reconstruction markets, not commodity export.

## 4.D Binding Constraints — Weighted and Data-Backed

Constraints are weighted by how decisive they are to unlocking the chain (1 = low, 5 = critical). Weights reflect the issues the firms themselves rank highest (NPA/UDC, 2025), backed by NPA/UDC's regional-competitiveness data. **Notably, existing players do not rank financing among their top five issues** — but capital is decisive specifically for the *upstream* (mining + sponge-iron) build-out. Cross-cutting constraints are elaborated in Chapter 7.

| # | Constraint (category) | Weight | Evidence / gravity |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Upstream feedstock & market gap (inputs)** | **5** | No commercial ironmaking from domestic ore; mills run on imported scrap/billet — the **most expensive route (~USD 676/t vs ~USD 478/t for domestic gas-DRI)**; ore exported raw (USD 35.8m); sponge-iron plants starved of ore |
| 2 | **Electricity reliability & cost (energy)** | **5** | The **#1 issue cited by firms**: instability and low quality. Uganda's industrial tariff **~8–10 US¢/kWh vs Kenya 5–7, Tanzania 6–8, Egypt 4–5** (NPA/UDC 2025); a dedicated extra-large tariff (~5.5¢, ERA) helps the biggest loads only |
| 3 | **Market access & standards enforcement (standards/market)** | **4** | Firms cite limited market and weak enforcement against substandard imports; idle capacity (only 30.5% utilised) is partly a demand-access problem |
| 4 | **Import competition (policy/market)** | **4** | Finished and semi-finished imports undercut domestic mills; flat steel (USD 219.5m) wholly imported |
| 5 | **Capital for upstream integration (finance)** | **4** | Not a top-five issue for existing players, but decisive for mining/sponge-iron; Uganda's commercial interest **18–23% — the highest among regional peers** (Kenya 13–17, Egypt 10–12, S. Africa 9–11) |
| 6 | **Tax-policy inconsistency (policy/regulatory)** | **3** | Firms cite inconsistent, customer-targeted tax measures (CPC letters from MoFPED targeting individual importers); VAT on coal inputs raises ironmaking cost |
| 7 | **Logistics (infrastructure)** | **3** | Transport ~USD 0.08–0.12/tonne-km (highest tier regionally); rail to ore deposits absent; ~18–20% of sale price |
| 8 | **Skills (skills)** | **3** | Metallurgical/process-engineering shortage; ~68% of manufacturers report difficulty finding technical skills (UMA) |
| 9 | **Technology & productivity (technology)** | **3** | Predominantly induction/re-rolling; little integrated DRI/EAF |

**Uganda's only regional cost advantage is labour** (~USD 150–250/worker, the lowest among peers); finance, electricity and logistics are all *more* expensive than in Kenya, Tanzania, Egypt or South Africa (NPA/UDC 2025). Competitiveness therefore depends on fixing power, securing domestic feedstock, and lowering the cost of capital for upstream investment — not on cheap labour alone.

## 4.E Greening & Sustainability

Green steel is a **competitiveness and market-access advantage** for Uganda, not just an environmental duty: the grid is hydro-dominant (low-carbon), and carbon-border measures (e.g. EU CBAM) increasingly penalise high-carbon steel in export markets.

| Dimension | Status / opportunity |
|---|---|
| **Current status** | Scrap-based induction melting (lower CO₂ than blast-furnace but energy-inefficient); slag, dust and scale managed under NEMA Effluent Discharge Regulations 2020 |
| **Current solutions** | Electric melting on a hydro grid; scrap recycling (circular by nature); coated coil/roofing extends asset life |
| **Emerging solutions (competitiveness enablers)** | (1) **Gas/green-hydrogen DRI** rather than coal — the NPA/UDC cost data already shows **gas-DRI (USD 262/t) beats coal-DRI (USD 299/t)**, so the green route is also the *cheap* route; (2) **scrap-based EAF on hydro power** for near-zero-carbon long products; (3) **slag valorisation** into cement/aggregate (links to the Cement chain); (4) **energy-efficiency and solar process heat** to cut the electricity cost that erodes competitiveness |

**Why it pays:** a hydro-powered, gas-DRI/scrap-EAF route lets Uganda market **low-carbon regional steel** — a differentiator in EAC/COMESA tenders and a hedge against carbon-border costs — while slag reuse turns a waste liability into saleable input. Here, green and cheapest coincide.

## 4.F Prioritized Products — through the Tenfold Lens

The prioritisation question: **which products build durable competitive advantage, link to Uganda's resource base, create jobs at scale, can grow with the regional/continental market, and are green by design — i.e. which get Uganda to tenfold growth sustainably?**

| Product / sub-sector | Market | Comp. adv. | Feasibility | Jobs | Import subst. | Green fit | Priority |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Sponge iron / DRI (ironmaking)** | High | High (350 Mt ore base) | Medium | High | High | High (gas/H₂) | **Priority** |
| **Flat products (hot/cold-rolled)** | High (USD 219m imports) | Medium | Medium | High | Very high | Medium | **Priority** |
| **Long products (rebar, sections)** | High (regional export) | High | High | High | Achieved | High (scrap-EAF) | **Priority (defend & expand)** |
| **Iron-ore mining & beneficiation** | Medium | High | Medium | Medium | Enabler | Medium | **Priority (enabler)** |
| Specialty / alloy steels | Low | Low | Low | Low | Low | — | Deprioritize |
| Tinplate / niche coated | Low | Low | Low | Low | Medium | — | Deprioritize (for now) |

**The three-to-four priorities for Iron & Steel:**

1. **Ironmaking (sponge iron/DRI) anchored on domestic ore** — the missing midstream step; unlocks the whole chain, stops raw-ore leakage, removes the ~30% imported-scrap cost penalty, and is the biggest lever toward tenfold-scale domestic steel. Tembo and Abyssinia exist but are ore-starved; NPA/UDC calls for a **≥1,000,000 tpa commercial iron-ore mine** to feed them.
2. **Flat-steel production** — the largest import line (USD 219.5m, 2024) with no domestic production; the clearest import-substitution prize and essential for machinery/vehicles in a tenfold economy.
3. **Long products (rebar/sections)** — already competitive and exporting regionally; defend and scale this proven, green (scrap-EAF) position.
4. **Iron-ore mining & beneficiation** — the enabling upstream step; NPA/UDC recommends Government lead via the Uganda National Mining Company (UNMC) or partner with private miners through UDC.

**Explicit deprioritization.** Uganda should **not** pursue specialty/alloy steels, large-diameter seamless pipe, or globally traded commodity flat steel for export at this stage — these need scale, technology and capital Uganda cannot competitively marshal now. Focus belongs on the integrated **ore→DRI→flat/long** pathway for domestic and regional markets.

## 4.G Project Profiles

Each profile is screened on three criteria simultaneously: **strategic alignment to tenfold growth, quick-win potential, and green design.**

**P1 — Commercial iron-ore mine (≥1,000,000 tpa) + sponge-iron supply (strategic anchor).**
- *Strategic:* removes the feedstock gap that starves Tembo/Abyssinia and underpins all downstream growth; NPA/UDC's first recommendation.
- *Quick-win element:* can begin by expanding an existing private miner via UDC, or scaling SINO Minerals (Kabale).
- *Green:* gas/green-H₂ DRI (also the lowest-cost route at USD 262/t); ore beneficiation on site.
- *Lead:* UNMC/UDC + private. *Indicative cost:* see Ch.8.

**P2 — Flat-rolling mill for import substitution (strategic).**
- *Strategic:* substitutes the USD 219.5m flat-steel import line; supplies machinery, vehicles, appliances.
- *Quick-win element:* a cold-rolling/coating line can start on imported coil and localise over time.
- *Green:* hydro-powered, energy-efficient lines.

**P3 — Scrap-based green EAF for long products (quick win + green).**
- *Strategic:* scales Uganda's proven rebar competitiveness for regional export.
- *Quick-win element:* shortest lead time; builds on existing players and idle capacity.
- *Green:* near-zero-carbon long products on the hydro grid — a low-carbon regional selling point.
