## 4.A Value Chain Map

The iron and steel value chain runs through six sequential phases, from iron-ore extraction to market distribution. Uganda has a substantial presence in the **downstream** phases (rolling and finishing) and a critical gap in the **upstream** phases (mining, ironmaking, and primary steelmaking) (NPA/UDC, 2025).

| Phase | Activity | Main inputs | Output |
|---|---|---|---|
| I | Exploration & mining | — | Beneficiated, graded iron ore |
| II | Ironmaking | Iron ore, coal/charcoal, dolomite, limestone | Sponge iron (DRI) / pig iron |
| III | Steelmaking | Sponge iron, pig iron, scrap | Crude steel |
| IV | Continuous casting | Liquid steel | Billets, blooms, slabs |
| V | Rolling & finishing | Billets, slabs, coil, wire rod | Long, flat, tubular, wire, structural & roofing products |
| VI | Market | Finished products | Domestic & regional sales |

**Where value is captured and lost.** Uganda's mills are concentrated in Phase V (rolling and finishing) and depend heavily on **imported semi-finished steel** (billets and slabs, HS 7207) and **scrap** (HS 7204) as feedstock, because domestic Phase I–III capacity is minimal. The clearest evidence of this structural gap is in the trade data: Uganda exported **USD 35.8 million of raw iron ore (HS 2601) in 2024, almost entirely to Kenya, while importing essentially none** (ITC TradeMap, HS 2601, 2024) — ore leaves the country unprocessed, then steel returns as imports. Conversely, Uganda imported **USD 78.5 million of semi-finished steel (HS 7207) in 2024**, up sharply from USD 43.0 million in 2023 (ITC TradeMap, HS 7207, 2024). The value-addition opportunity lies in closing the upstream gap.

**Indicative value addition by phase** (per tonne of steel, illustrative at ~USD 750/t; NPA/UDC 2025):

| Phase | Incremental value addition |
|---|---|
| Mining & beneficiation | 5–10% |
| Ironmaking (DRI) | 15–25% |
| Steelmaking | 20–30% |
| Continuous casting | 10–15% |
| Rolling & finishing | 10–20% |

The single largest value-addition steps — ironmaking and steelmaking — are precisely the steps Uganda has barely begun.

## 4.B Current State Assessment

**Installed capacity and utilization.** The NPA/UDC (2025) assessment records total installed capacity of approximately **1.68 million tonnes per annum (tpa)** across the industry, operating at roughly **30% utilization** — meaning around two-thirds of existing capacity is idle, even before any new investment. Low utilization is driven by feedstock cost, energy cost, and competition from imports.

**Firms and structure.** The industry is concentrated in downstream rolling and finishing, with an estimated 40+ firms across the chain (NPA/UDC 2025). Leading players include:

| Firm | Role | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Tembo Steel (U) Ltd | Closest to integrated production | Operates Uganda's main sponge-iron (DRI) capacity; commissioned a second DRI plant in January 2025 (~250,000 t), the only producer reaching upstream (State House Uganda, 2025) |
| Roofings Ltd / Roofings Rolling Mills | Long products, wire, roofing, coated sheet | ~1,850 permanent employees (2021); two integrated entities (African Business, 2021) |
| Steel & Tube Industries Ltd (STIL) | Long products, tubes, sections | ~1,080 permanent staff; ~6,000 total beneficiaries including the wider value chain (Uganda Development Bank) |
| Uganda Baati Ltd | Roofing & coated sheet | Leading roofing-sheet manufacturer |
| BM Steel, Pramukh, Sembule, Yogi Steels, Abyssinia, others | Rolling, sections, wire | Downstream finishing |

**Employment.** The chain is a significant industrial employer, with the larger firms each employing in the high hundreds to low thousands, and substantial indirect employment in construction, fabrication, and distribution (UDB; NPA/UDC 2025).

**Trade position (ITC TradeMap, 2024).** The trade data tells the core story of import dependence in the upstream and flat-steel segments, and competitiveness in the long-products segment:

| Segment (HS) | Imports 2024 | Exports 2024 | Reading |
|---|---|---|---|
| Flat-rolled, hot-rolled (7208) | USD 219.5m (China, Japan, Korea) | USD 2.9m | Large deficit — **no domestic flat-rolling** |
| Bars & rods in coil (7213) | USD 34.1m (China, Egypt, S. Africa) | USD 0.5m | Net importer of wire rod |
| Other bars & rods (7214 — rebar) | USD 2.7m | **USD 55.8m** (S. Sudan, DRC, Kenya) | **Net exporter — competitive** |
| Semi-finished (7207, billet/slab) | USD 78.5m (S. Africa, China, Oman) | USD 2.5m | Large deficit — **no primary steelmaking** |
| Ferrous scrap (7204) | USD 72.8m (S. Sudan, Kenya, UK) | negligible | Feedstock dependence on imported scrap |
| Iron ore (2601) | ~0 | **USD 35.8m** (Kenya) | **Raw ore exported unprocessed** |

The pattern is unambiguous: Uganda is **competitive in finished long products (rebar)** — exporting some USD 56 million in 2024, mostly to South Sudan and the DRC — but is a **major net importer of the upstream and flat-steel segments** it cannot yet produce, and it **exports its iron ore raw**.

**2025 investment momentum.** Two developments materially change the outlook and should be reflected in prioritization:
- **Tembo Steel** commissioned a second DRI (sponge iron) plant in January 2025, adding roughly 250,000 t of ironmaking capacity — the only firm pushing into Phase II (State House Uganda, 2025).
- **Devki Group** broke ground in November 2025 on a USD 500 million vertically integrated steel complex (Tororo/Mbarara), projected to create around 15,000 jobs — a potential step-change toward integrated, ore-based production (GMK Center, 2025; The Independent, 2025).

## 4.C Binding Constraints Analysis

| Dimension | Assessment for Iron & Steel |
|---|---|
| **Inputs / raw materials** | High-grade iron-ore deposits exist (Muko, south-west; Mutaka) but commercial-scale mining and beneficiation are minimal. Mills run on imported billets, slabs, and scrap. Domestic scrap is insufficient and partly imported (USD 72.8m, 2024). |
| **Technology & productivity** | Predominantly induction-furnace and re-rolling technology; little DRI/EAF integrated capacity. Productivity and energy efficiency lag regional integrated producers. Tembo's DRI expansion is the main exception. |
| **Energy** | Electricity is a decisive cost in steel. The extra-large industrial tariff is ~5.5 US¢/kWh (5¢ off-peak) in 2025 (ERA), competitive regionally but reliability and the cost of backup remain issues. |
| **Water** | Process and cooling water needed; managed under NEMA/NWSC frameworks; not the binding constraint. |
| **Logistics** | Landlocked-cost penalty is severe: national logistics inefficiency costs the economy ~USD 827m/yr and logistics is ~18–20% of the sale price of goods (sector estimates) — heavy for a bulk, low-value-density product like steel. |
| **Waste & effluent** | Slag, dust, and scale managed under NEMA Effluent Discharge Regulations 2020; circular-economy potential in slag reuse. |
| **Finance & investment** | Integrated steel is highly capital-intensive; long-term finance is scarce and costly, with 2025 liquidity tightening crowding out private credit (KPMG Budget Brief 2025/26). |
| **Skills & workforce** | Shortage of metallurgical and process-engineering skills; ~68% of manufacturers report difficulty finding required technical skills (Uganda Manufacturers Association, UMA). |
| **Standards & quality** | UNBS standards and Q-Mark apply; substandard imports and uneven enforcement distort the market. |
| **Policy & regulatory** | EAC Common External Tariff and import policy shape competitiveness; protection of finished products without securing feedstock can raise downstream costs. Mining licensing and land access constrain upstream entry. |

## 4.D Market Assessment

**Domestic.** Demand is driven by construction and infrastructure and is growing. Uganda already meets domestic demand for many **long products** (rebar, sections) but imports virtually all **flat products** and **semi-finished feedstock**.

**Regional (EAC/COMESA).** Uganda is a competitive exporter of rebar and long products to **South Sudan and eastern DRC** (USD 55.8m of HS 7214 in 2024), markets where proximity offsets the landlocked penalty (ITC TradeMap, 2024). These are durable, growing markets with reconstruction and construction demand. The EAC market is ~USD 4.8 billion in intra-regional trade and growing (EAC, 2025).

**Global.** Uganda is not, and will not soon be, a globally competitive steel exporter. Global benchmarks (World Steel Association, 2025) confirm scale economics that Uganda cannot match upstream; the realistic horizon is domestic substitution plus regional export of finished products.

**Accessible market conclusion.** The realistically accessible market is (1) **domestic flat-steel and semi-finished substitution** and (2) **regional finished long-product exports**. Global commodity-steel export is not accessible.

## 4.E Prioritized Products

Applying the five-criterion scoring framework (Chapter 3) to the chain:

| Product / sub-sector | Market | Comp. adv. | Feasibility | Jobs | Import subst. | Priority |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Sponge iron / DRI (ironmaking)** | High | High (ore base) | Medium | High | High | **Priority** |
| **Flat products (hot/cold-rolled)** | High (USD 219m imports) | Medium | Medium | High | Very high | **Priority** |
| **Long products (rebar, sections)** | High (regional export) | High | High | High | Achieved | **Priority (defend & expand)** |
| **Iron-ore mining & beneficiation** | Medium | High | Medium | Medium | Enabler | **Priority (enabler)** |
| Specialty / alloy steels | Low | Low | Low | Low | Low | Deprioritize |
| Tinplate / niche coated | Low | Low | Low | Low | Medium | Deprioritize (for now) |

**The three-to-four priorities for Iron & Steel:**

1. **Ironmaking (sponge iron/DRI) anchored on domestic ore** — the missing midstream step; unlocks the whole chain and stops raw-ore leakage. Tembo's expansion and Devki's integrated plant show commercial appetite.
2. **Flat-steel production** — the single largest import line (USD 219.5m in 2024) with no domestic production; the clearest import-substitution prize.
3. **Long products (rebar/sections)** — already competitive and exporting regionally; defend and scale this proven position.
4. **Iron-ore mining & beneficiation** — the enabling upstream step that must accompany ironmaking.

**Explicit deprioritization.** Uganda should **not** pursue specialty/alloy steels, large-diameter seamless pipe, or globally traded commodity flat steel for export at this stage — these require scale, technology, and capital Uganda cannot competitively marshal now. Focus belongs on the integrated ore→DRI→flat/long pathway for domestic and regional markets.

## 4.F Priority Action Matrix

| Timeframe | Action | Lead | Indicative cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Quick win (0–12m)** | Enforce UNBS standards against substandard steel imports; level the playing field for compliant domestic mills | UNBS, MTIC, Uganda Revenue Authority (URA) | Low (administrative) |
| Quick win (0–12m) | Restrict or tax raw iron-ore exports (HS 2601) to retain feedstock for domestic ironmaking | Ministry of Energy and Mineral Development (MEMD), Ministry of Finance, Planning and Economic Development (MoFPED) | Low (policy) |
| **Reform (1–3y)** | Secure iron-ore mining licences and beneficiation at Muko/Mutaka; resolve land access | MEMD/DGSM, MTIC | Medium |
| Reform (1–3y) | Power agreement for extra-large steel loads; reliability guarantees | ERA, UEDCL, MEMD | Medium |
| Reform (1–3y) | Skills programme in metallurgy/process engineering with Uganda Industrial Research Institute (UIRI), Makerere, Technical and Vocational Education and Training (TVET) | Ministry of Education and Sports (MoES), UIRI | Medium |
| **Investment (3–5y)** | Integrated ore→DRI→steel→flat-rolling capacity (e.g. Devki complex; Tembo expansion) | Private-led + PPP | Indicative USD 0.5–1.0bn (see Ch.8) |
| Investment (3–5y) | Flat-rolling mill to substitute HS 7208 imports | Private-led | Indicative (see Ch.8) |
