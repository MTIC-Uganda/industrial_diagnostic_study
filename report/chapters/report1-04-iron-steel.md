## 4.0 Why Iron & Steel Matters for the Tenfold Growth Strategy

Steel is the physical backbone of a ten-times-larger economy. Every pillar of the Tenfold Growth Strategy — industrialisation, infrastructure, urbanisation, and agro-processing — consumes steel: rebar and sections for buildings and bridges, plate and coil for machinery and vehicles, wire and roofing for housing and agriculture. **A country cannot grow its economy tenfold while importing the steel that growth is built from.** Uganda today consumes on the order of 1 million tonnes of steel a year but produces little of it from domestic ore; a tenfold economy will demand several million tonnes annually (Section 4.C). Closing that gap domestically is therefore not a sector ambition — it is a precondition for the national one.

This chapter diagnoses the chain in the order a diagnosis should run: **what exists today (4.B) → what the market will demand to 2040 (4.C) → what is holding the chain back (4.D) → how it can grow green (4.E) → what to prioritise and build (4.F–4.H).**

## 4.A Value Chain Map

The iron and steel value chain runs through six sequential phases, from iron-ore extraction to market distribution. Uganda has a substantial presence in the **downstream** phases (rolling and finishing) and a critical gap in the **upstream** phases (mining, ironmaking, and primary steelmaking) (TWG engagements; NPA/UDC, 2025).

| Phase | Activity | Main inputs | Output | Uganda's position |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| I | Exploration & mining | — | Beneficiated, graded iron ore | **Gap** — minimal commercial mining |
| II | Ironmaking | Iron ore, coal/charcoal, dolomite, limestone | Sponge iron (DRI) / pig iron | **Emerging** — Tembo only |
| III | Steelmaking | Sponge iron, pig iron, scrap | Crude steel | **Weak** — scrap-based induction only |
| IV | Continuous casting | Liquid steel | Billets, blooms, slabs | **Partial** — some billet casting |
| V | Rolling & finishing | Billets, slabs, coil, wire rod | Long, flat, tubular, wire, structural & roofing products | **Strong (long products); absent (flat)** |
| VI | Market | Finished products | Domestic & regional sales | **Active** — domestic + S. Sudan/DRC export |

**Where value is captured and lost.** Uganda's mills are concentrated in Phase V and depend heavily on **imported semi-finished steel** (billets and slabs, HS 7207) and **scrap** (HS 7204) as feedstock, because domestic Phase I–III capacity is minimal. The clearest evidence of this structural gap is in the trade data: Uganda exported **USD 35.8 million of raw iron ore (HS 2601) in 2024, almost entirely to Kenya, while importing essentially none** (ITC TradeMap, 2024) — ore leaves the country unprocessed, then steel returns as imports. Conversely, Uganda imported **USD 78.5 million of semi-finished steel (HS 7207) in 2024**, up sharply from USD 43.0 million in 2023 (ITC TradeMap, 2024). The value-addition opportunity lies in closing the upstream gap.

**Indicative value addition by phase** (per tonne of steel, illustrative at ~USD 750/t; NPA/UDC 2025). The percentages are each phase's share of finished value — read as a build-up toward the final product, not a precise cost breakdown:

| Phase | Incremental value addition | ≈ USD/tonne |
|---|---|---|
| Mining & beneficiation | 5–10% | ~38–75 |
| Ironmaking (DRI) | 15–25% | ~113–188 |
| Steelmaking | 20–30% | ~150–225 |
| Continuous casting | 10–15% | ~75–113 |
| Rolling & finishing | 10–20% | ~75–150 |

The single largest value-addition steps — **ironmaking and steelmaking (together ~35–55% of finished value)** — are precisely the steps Uganda has barely begun. This is where the tenfold opportunity concentrates.

**Installed capacity and utilization.** The NPA/UDC (2025) assessment records total installed capacity of approximately **1.68 million tonnes per annum (tpa)**, operating at roughly **30% utilization** — around two-thirds of existing capacity is idle even before new investment. Low utilization is driven by feedstock cost, energy cost, and import competition. Raising utilisation toward 70–80% is itself a near-term win requiring no new mills.

The pattern is unambiguous: Uganda is **competitive in finished long products (rebar)** — exporting ~USD 56 million in 2024, mostly to South Sudan and the DRC — but is a **major net importer of the upstream and flat-steel segments** it cannot yet produce, and it **exports its iron ore raw**.

### Investment momentum (2025)

- **Tembo Steel** commissioned a second DRI (sponge iron) plant in January 2025, adding ~250,000 t of ironmaking capacity — the only firm in Phase II (State House Uganda, 2025).
- **Devki Group** broke ground in November 2025 on a **USD 500 million** vertically integrated steel complex (Tororo/Mbarara), projected to create ~15,000 jobs — a potential step-change toward integrated, ore-based production (GMK Center, 2025; The Independent, 2025).

Together these signal real commercial appetite for the exact upstream steps the chain is missing — the foundation any tenfold-scale plan should build on.

## 4.C Market Analysis (four levels) and 2040 Projection

### Current market by level

| Level | Market size / Uganda position | Growth drivers |
|---|---|---|
| **Uganda (domestic)** | ~0.9–1.1 Mtpa apparent steel consumption (confirm: UBOS); met by domestic long products + heavy imports of flat & semi-finished | Construction, infrastructure (roads, energy, housing), urbanisation |
| **Regional (EAC + COMESA)** | EAC intra-regional trade ~USD 4.8bn and growing; Uganda competitive in rebar to S. Sudan & eastern DRC (USD 55.8m, 2024) | Reconstruction (S. Sudan, DRC), regional construction, CET protection |
| **Continental (Africa)** | African steel demand ~35–40 Mtpa, large structural deficit met by imports; AfCFTA opens access | Africa-wide infrastructure deficit, AfCFTA tariff liberalisation |
| **Global** | ~1.9 billion tonnes/yr; dominated by China; commodity scale economics Uganda cannot match upstream | Not realistically accessible for Ugandan commodity steel export |

### 2040 projection — Uganda on the Tenfold logic

The question is not "current demand × 10." It is: *if Uganda's economy is ten times larger by 2040, driven by industrialisation, infrastructure and urbanisation, what steel demand does that create?* Steel intensity rises with income and then plateaus. Uganda's apparent steel use is **~20–25 kg per capita** today, far below the world average (~230 kg) and below lower-middle-income peers (~100–150 kg). A tenfold economy implies structural transformation that pulls per-capita consumption toward peer levels.

| Driver | Today | 2040 (tenfold logic) |
|---|---|---|
| Per-capita steel use | ~20–25 kg | ~90–130 kg (peer convergence) |
| Population | ~46–48m | ~60m+ |
| **Implied annual steel demand** | **~1 Mtpa** | **~5.5–8 Mtpa** |

**Strategic implication:** Uganda must plan now to *create and secure* a 5–8 Mtpa steel market — first by substituting today's imports (flat + semi-finished), then by anchoring integrated, ore-based capacity ahead of demand. **Other markets** (regional, continental, global) are projected on business-as-usual growth (~3–5%/yr regionally), where Uganda's realistic role is finished long products to neighbouring reconstruction markets, not commodity export.

## 4.D Binding Constraints — Weighted

Constraints are categorised, weighted by how decisive they are to unlocking the chain (1 = low, 5 = critical), and evidenced. Cross-cutting constraints (energy, logistics, finance, skills) are elaborated further in Chapter 7.

| # | Constraint (category) | Weight | Evidence / gravity |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Feedstock & upstream gap (inputs)** | **5** | No commercial ironmaking from domestic ore; mills run on USD 78.5m imported billet + USD 72.8m imported scrap (2024); ore exported raw (USD 35.8m) |
| 2 | **Finance / capital (finance)** | **5** | Integrated steel is highly capital-intensive (USD 0.5–1.0bn class); long-term finance scarce, 2025 liquidity tightening crowding out private credit (KPMG 2025/26) |
| 3 | **Energy cost & reliability (energy)** | **4** | Electricity is decisive in melting; ~5.5 US¢/kWh (ERA 2025) is regionally competitive but reliability and backup cost erode it |
| 4 | **Technology & productivity (technology)** | **4** | Predominantly induction/re-rolling; little DRI/EAF integration; productivity lags regional integrated producers |
| 5 | **Logistics (infrastructure)** | **4** | Landlocked penalty severe for bulk, low-value-density steel; logistics ~18–20% of sale price; ~USD 827m/yr national logistics inefficiency (sector estimates) |
| 6 | **Skills (skills)** | **3** | Metallurgical/process-engineering shortage; ~68% of manufacturers report difficulty finding technical skills (UMA) |
| 7 | **Standards & enforcement (standards)** | **3** | UNBS standards/Q-Mark exist but substandard imports and uneven enforcement distort the market |
| 8 | **Policy coherence (policy/regulatory)** | **3** | EAC CET and protection of finished products without securing feedstock can raise downstream costs; mining licensing & land access constrain upstream entry |
| 9 | **Water (inputs)** | **1** | Process/cooling water managed under NEMA/NWSC; not binding |

**The two critical constraints (weight 5)** — the upstream feedstock gap and access to long-term capital — are mutually reinforcing: closing the feedstock gap *requires* the very capital that is scarce. Any credible plan must solve both together, which is why the integrated investments now emerging (Tembo, Devki) matter so much.

## 4.E Greening & Sustainability

Green steel is not only an environmental obligation — for Uganda it is a **competitiveness and market-access advantage**, because Uganda's grid is hydro-dominant (low-carbon) and because carbon-border measures (e.g. EU CBAM) increasingly penalise high-carbon steel in export markets.

| Dimension | Status / opportunity |
|---|---|
| **Current status** | Scrap-based induction melting (lower CO₂ than blast-furnace, but energy-inefficient); slag, dust and scale managed under NEMA Effluent Discharge Regulations 2020 |
| **Current solutions** | Electric melting on a hydro grid; some scrap recycling (circular by nature); roofing from coated coil extends asset life |
| **Emerging solutions (competitiveness enablers)** | (1) **DRI using natural gas or green hydrogen** rather than coal — aligns with Tembo's DRI route and future Kabaale gas; (2) **scrap-based EAF** powered by hydro for near-zero-carbon long products; (3) **slag valorisation** into cement/aggregate (links to the Cement chain); (4) **solar-assisted process heat** and energy-efficiency retrofits to cut the energy cost that erodes competitiveness |

**Why it pays:** a hydro-powered, scrap/DRI-EAF route lets Uganda credibly market **low-carbon regional steel** — a differentiator in EAC/COMESA tenders and a hedge against carbon-border costs, while slag reuse turns a waste liability into a saleable input.

## 4.F Prioritized Products — through the Tenfold Lens

The prioritisation question is: **which products build durable competitive advantage, link to Uganda's resource base, create jobs at scale, can grow with the regional/continental market, and are green by design — i.e. which get Uganda to tenfold growth sustainably?**

| Product / sub-sector | Market | Comp. adv. | Feasibility | Jobs | Import subst. | Green fit | Priority |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Sponge iron / DRI (ironmaking)** | High | High (ore base) | Medium | High | High | High (gas/H₂/hydro) | **Priority** |
| **Flat products (hot/cold-rolled)** | High (USD 219m imports) | Medium | Medium | High | Very high | Medium | **Priority** |
| **Long products (rebar, sections)** | High (regional export) | High | High | High | Achieved | High (scrap-EAF) | **Priority (defend & expand)** |
| **Iron-ore mining & beneficiation** | Medium | High | Medium | Medium | Enabler | Medium | **Priority (enabler)** |
| Specialty / alloy steels | Low | Low | Low | Low | Low | — | Deprioritize |
| Tinplate / niche coated | Low | Low | Low | Low | Medium | — | Deprioritize (for now) |

**The three-to-four priorities for Iron & Steel:**

1. **Ironmaking (sponge iron/DRI) anchored on domestic ore** — the missing midstream step; unlocks the whole chain, stops raw-ore leakage, and is the single biggest lever toward tenfold-scale domestic steel. Tembo's expansion and Devki's integrated plant show commercial appetite.
2. **Flat-steel production** — the largest import line (USD 219.5m, 2024) with no domestic production; the clearest import-substitution prize and essential for machinery/vehicles in a tenfold economy.
3. **Long products (rebar/sections)** — already competitive and exporting regionally; defend and scale this proven, green (scrap-EAF) position.
4. **Iron-ore mining & beneficiation** — the enabling upstream step that must accompany ironmaking.

**Explicit deprioritization.** Uganda should **not** pursue specialty/alloy steels, large-diameter seamless pipe, or globally traded commodity flat steel for export at this stage — these require scale, technology, and capital Uganda cannot competitively marshal now. Focus belongs on the integrated **ore→DRI→flat/long** pathway for domestic and regional markets.

## 4.G Project Profiles

Each profile is screened on three criteria simultaneously: **strategic alignment to tenfold growth, quick-win potential, and green/sustainable design.**

**P1 — Integrated ore→DRI→steel complex (strategic anchor).**
- *Strategic:* the backbone project for tenfold-scale domestic steel; converts Muko/Mutaka ore into sponge iron and crude steel.
- *Quick-win element:* phase via Tembo's existing DRI expansion and Devki's complex already underway.
- *Green:* gas/green-hydrogen DRI + hydro-EAF; slag valorisation to cement.
- *Indicative cost:* USD 0.5–1.0bn class (see Ch.8). *Lead:* private-led + PPP.

**P2 — Flat-rolling mill for import substitution (strategic + quick-ish).**
- *Strategic:* substitutes the USD 219.5m flat-steel import line; supplies machinery/vehicle/appliance manufacturing.
- *Quick-win element:* a cold-rolling/coating line can be stood up faster than full integration, initially on imported coil, localising over time.
- *Green:* hydro-powered; energy-efficient lines.

**P3 — Scrap-based green EAF for long products (quick win + green).**
- *Strategic:* scales Uganda's proven rebar competitiveness for regional export.
- *Quick-win element:* shortest lead time; builds on existing players.
- *Green:* near-zero-carbon long products on the hydro grid — a low-carbon regional selling point.
