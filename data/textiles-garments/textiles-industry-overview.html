Sector Challenges

Second-Hand Clothes (Mitumba)

Imports of used clothing reached USD 106 million in 2022, up 43% from 2013. This price competition is widely cited as the primary suppressor of local textile demand. President Museveni reaffirmed a ban commitment in early 2024, but implementation remains delayed due to trader opposition and diplomatic pressure.

Electricity — Cost & Reliability

All three industrial parks reported electricity as their top challenge. Power tariffs exceed the promised USc 5/kWhr. Frequent blackouts cause production halts and machine damage. UMEME reportedly raised utilization thresholds, eliminating lower-tariff access for most parks.

Imported Fabric Dependency

Almost all garment factories in industrial parks use imported woven fabric — primarily from China. Locally produced yarn is limited to knitted fabrics. No locally produced woven fabric manufacturer operates at scale, forcing the industry to import most of its upstream inputs.

Poor Road Access to Parks

Access roads to Liao Shen (Semuto Road under construction as of 2024) and MMP (road construction started July 2022, still unfinished) cause supply chain delays. Only Sino-Mbale had tarmac access at the time of the 2024 MTIC visits, though internal park roads were also unfinished.

High Labour Turnover

Factories predominantly employ casual labour. Workers trained in garment skills — especially tailors and machine operators — frequently leave abruptly, reducing factory efficiency. Labour mobility is high because the skills are transferable and informal tailoring is widely available.

AGOA Suspension & Export Market Access

Uganda's suspension from the African Growth and Opportunity Act (AGOA) over domestic policy issues removed duty-free access to the US market — a key advantage for garment exporters. This particularly affects GOKTA Workwear, whose European and US export model relies on Uganda's preferential trade status.

Low Market Awareness

MTIC field teams found that most products from industrial parks are not widely available in Ugandan retail markets. Consumers and traders are largely unaware of what is manufactured domestically. The MTIC recommended an exhibition program and trader–manufacturer linkage meetings.

Cotton Value Chain Gap

Uganda exported approximately 98,000 MT of cotton lint in 2022, while processing less than 10% locally. From a peak of 120,000 tonnes in the late 1960s, domestic cotton production now sits at ~30,000 tonnes. Policy targets five new vertically integrated mills to raise local processing and add 50,000 jobs.

Policy & Investment Context

Three overarching national frameworks govern how Uganda's textile and garment sector is prioritised and resourced. They are presented here in descending order of authority — the Tenfold Growth Strategy sets the supreme long-term vision; the National Development Plan IV translates that into a five-year operational programme; and the National Industrial Policy provides the sector-specific implementation framework.

①
Tenfold Growth Strategy (2023–2040) — Supreme Framework
Ministry of Finance, Planning & Economic Development · Launched FY 2023/24 · Execution commenced FY 2025/26
Vision & Mandate

President Museveni announced in January 2024 that Uganda must expand its GDP tenfold — from USD 50 billion (FY 2023/24 baseline) to USD 500 billion by 2040. This is Uganda's supreme economic planning document. It supersedes all sectoral plans by setting the outer ambition that all other policies must serve. FY 2025/26 marks the first year of formal execution.

Four ATMS Pillars — Where Textiles Fit

The strategy is anchored on four named sectors: Agro-industrialisation (A), Tourism (T), Mineral-based industrial development (M), and Science & Technology Innovation (S). Cotton, textiles and garments fall explicitly under the Agro-industrialisation pillar — described in the official strategy document as Phase 4 of Uganda's economic evolution: "value addition through manufacturing of the expanded commodities basket," explicitly listing cotton as one of the priority commodities.

Five Phases of Uganda's Economic Evolution (as defined in the Strategy)

The strategy contextualises Uganda's growth through five phases: Phase 1 (recovery of the colonial 3Cs economy — Cotton, Coffee, Copper); Phase 2 (infrastructure and human capital); Phase 3 (services and monetisation); Phase 4 (value addition through manufacturing — cotton, fruits, milk, tea, timber, sugar); Phase 5 (knowledge economy — pathogens, automobiles, 4th Industrial Revolution). Uganda is currently transitioning from Phase 3 into Phase 4, making textile manufacturing investment directly aligned with the supreme national strategy.

Implication for the Textile Sector

The Tenfold Strategy explicitly requires Uganda to stop exporting raw cotton lint and begin processing it into yarn, fabric and finished garments. Each step up the value chain multiplies export value: lint earns USD 1.4/kg; yarn USD 3/kg; fabric USD 5/kg; garments USD 8–10/kg. Achieving the export targets — USD 250B in merchandise exports by 2040 — is impossible without a fully developed cotton–textile–apparel value chain. The strategy also sets the energy investment targets (4–10× increase in generation capacity) that would directly resolve the electricity bottleneck crippling industrial parks today.

②
Fourth National Development Plan — NDP IV (2025/26–2029/30)
National Planning Authority · Approved by Parliament January 9, 2025 · Launched by President Museveni June 5, 2025
Role & Status

NDP IV is Uganda's five-year operational blueprint, the second in the series of six National Development Plans designed to deliver the Tenfold Growth target. It runs from FY 2025/26 to FY 2029/30 under the theme: "Sustainable Industrialisation for Inclusive Growth, Employment and Wealth Creation." It explicitly describes its mandate as delivering the first phase of tenfold growth — doubling the size of the economy within the plan period.

Five Strategic Pillars Relevant to Textiles

1. Agro-industrialisation: Explicitly targets value addition to agricultural products — development of value-addition infrastructure, strengthened post-harvest handling, enhanced competitiveness. Cotton is a named priority crop; textiles and apparels are a named priority agro-industrial value chain.

2. Value Addition & Industrialisation: Factories using locally available raw materials are a presidential priority. NDP IV mandates accelerating establishment of such factories — directly targeted at upgrading Uganda's lint-to-garment pipeline.

3. Strategic Infrastructure: Investment in transport, energy, water and ICT to support industrialisation. This directly addresses the road access and electricity challenges flagged by MTIC during the 2024 industrial park visits.

4. Private Sector-Led Growth: NDP IV frames the private sector as the primary engine of growth, with government providing an enabling environment. Industrial parks like Sino-Mbale and Liao Shen, driven by private developers and Chinese FDI, are consistent with this model.

5. Human Capital & Skills: Industrial vocational hubs are a key priority — directly addressing the skills gap and high labour turnover seen in garment factories, where trained tailors frequently leave for informal work.

Textile-Specific Relevance

NDP IV explicitly lists cotton, textiles and apparels as a priority agro-industrial sub-sector. It notes that the Uganda Police Force and Uganda Prison Services engage in leather and textile manufacturing but their capacity remains underutilised — requiring mechanisation, R&D and technology transfer. It also identifies the MTIC-flagged challenge of NDP IV period needing to address SHC (second-hand clothes) policy, AfCFTA market access, and the BUBU framework as key enablers for local textile producers.

③
National Industrial Policy 2020 (NIP 2020) — Sector Implementation Framework
Ministry of Trade, Industry & Cooperatives · Cabinet approved 2020 · Launched May 4, 2021 (with UNDP support) · Effective 10-year horizon to 2029/30
Background & Purpose

NIP 2020 is a review and upgrade of the National Industrial Policy 2008. It provides the sector-specific implementation framework that operationalises NDP targets at the factory and value-chain level. It consolidates achievements from NIP 2008 and gives new direction to industrial development for the decade to 2030. It was developed with UNDP technical support and launched by Prime Minister Ruhakana Rugunda in May 2021.

Three Priority Industry Clusters

1. Agro-Based Industries (includes Textiles): Fruits, Coffee, Tea, Cassava, Cotton, Textile & Apparels, Oil Seeds, Bananas, Grains, Sugarcane, Dairy, Leather & Leather Products. The NIP explicitly selects these through "extensive study and consultation" as the value chains with the highest immediate potential for value addition, employment and import substitution.

2. Extractive Industries: Iron & Steel, Cement, Oil & Gas, Salt, Fertilizers — sectors that create enabling inputs for textile machinery and construction of industrial parks.

3. Knowledge-Based Industries: Automobiles, Pharmaceuticals, Electrical & Electronic Products — Phase 5 aspirations consistent with Tenfold Strategy's vision of a future knowledge economy.

Six Strategic Objectives of NIP 2020

① Increase and sustain supply of quality raw materials for value addition — directly targeting the cotton lint export problem. ② Develop and strengthen skilled human resources — addressing the labour turnover crisis in garment factories. ③ Accelerate R&D and adoption of appropriate technologies. ④ Promote resource-efficient and environmentally sustainable industrialisation — relevant to Hangda Textile's wood-fired boiler risk flagged by MTIC. ⑤ Lower the cost of manufacturing, especially energy — directly addressing the electricity tariff grievances of industrial parks. ⑥ Improve access to regional markets — relevant to AGOA, AfCFTA and EAC market development for garment exporters.

Relationship to National Textile Policy

NIP 2020 sits above the National Textile Policy (2008, updated 2020) in the policy hierarchy. The Textile Policy is a sub-sectoral implementation instrument for the cotton–textile–apparel value chain, while NIP 2020 is the economy-wide industrial framework. The Textile Policy's specific targets — five new integrated mills, mitumba phase-out, local fabric production — are consistent with but subordinate to NIP 2020's economy-wide manufacturing value-addition goals.

Supporting Policy Instruments

National Textile Policy (2008 / updated 2020)

The sector-specific policy under NIP 2020. Targets a complete cotton–textile–apparel value chain, reduced dependence on imported fabric, five new vertically integrated mills over eight years, and a phase-out of second-hand clothes. Explicitly identifies mitumba imports, counterfeits and lack of local fabric production as the three primary threats to sector development.

BUBU — Buy Uganda Build Uganda

Requires public institutions to prioritise locally manufactured goods in procurement. Textile firms including Lurtex and Fine Spinners explicitly reference this policy. The UPDF uniform manufacturing JV (NEC-MMP at MMP park) is a direct application — though MTIC notes Uganda still imports UPDF uniforms from Asia despite Nytil's capacity to supply them.

Industrial Park Investment Model

Uganda operates 22 state-linked industrial parks, many under a tripartite model: Government (landlord) → Developer (Chinese firm) → Tenant factories. Sino-Mbale (Tian Tang Group / Hebei Province) and Liao Shen (Zhang Group, USD 400M) exemplify the model. The park model is formally supported under NDP IV's private sector-led growth pillar.

Import Tariff Protection

Government raised import duty on textile fabrics from 25% to 35% under NIP 2020 objectives to protect domestic producers. Paired with presidential commitment (reaffirmed early 2024) to eventually ban mitumba imports — delayed due to trader opposition and past US diplomatic pressure linked to AGOA. Policy under review for phased implementation under NDP IV.

Free Trade Zones (UFZA)

The Uganda Free Zones Authority has issued developer licences including to Shree Modern Textiles (81-acre free port in Jinja — ginning, weaving and apparel, projected 1,500 direct / 4,500 indirect jobs). Free zones offer tax relief and are a key instrument for attracting export-oriented garment FDI consistent with the Tenfold Strategy's export targets.

Uganda Cotton Development Organisation (UCDO)

UCDO oversees the ginning and primary processing layer of the value chain. Around 40 ginneries operate nationally. Most lint is exported to Asian buyers — only ~10% is processed locally. Under the Tenfold Strategy and NDP IV, UCDO's role is expected to shift from managing lint exports to facilitating domestic value addition and linking ginneries to spinning mills.
